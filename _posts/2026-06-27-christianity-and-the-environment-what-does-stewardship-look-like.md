Christians sometimes talk about the environment as if it is a side issue. It can sound like something for scientists, activists, politicians or people who spend their weekends composting. For others, environmental language sounds suspiciously like a substitute religion, where “saving the planet” replaces worshipping the Creator. I understand that concern. Christians do not worship creation. We worship the God who made heaven and earth.

Yet precisely because God is Creator, Christians cannot treat creation as disposable. The opening chapters of Genesis do not present the world as an accident, a machine or a pile of raw materials waiting for human exploitation. Creation is God’s good world. Human beings are placed within it, not above it as independent owners, but as image-bearers called to exercise wise, responsible care.

That means environmental concern is not an optional political add-on to Christian faith. It is part of discipleship. The question is not whether Christians should care about creation, but what faithful creation care actually looks like.

## The World Belongs to God

The Bible’s first claim about the world is not that it belongs to humanity, but that it belongs to God. “In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth” is a theological claim before it is anything else. The earth is not self-owned, and neither are we.

Psalm 24:1 says, “The earth is the LORD’s and all that is in it.” That does not leave much room for the idea that creation is ours to use without restraint. We receive the world as a gift, but also as a trust.

Genesis 1 speaks of humanity being made in the image of God and given dominion over the creatures. That word “dominion” has sometimes been used to justify exploitation, but in the biblical story human rule is supposed to reflect God’s rule. God’s rule is not greedy, wasteful or destructive. It is generous, life-giving and ordered toward flourishing.

Genesis 2:15 is especially important. The man is placed in the garden “to till it and keep it.” The language carries the sense of working and guarding, cultivating and protecting. Human beings are not called to leave creation untouched, as though any human activity is a problem. We are called to cultivate creation in a way that protects its goodness.

That is stewardship. Stewardship means we are not owners in the absolute sense. We are entrusted with something that belongs to another. We may use it, develop it and benefit from it, but not as though there will never be an accounting.

## Stewardship Is Not the Same as Preservation Alone

Christian stewardship is not simply “do not touch anything.” Human beings are creative, productive and cultural creatures. Farming, building, technology, medicine, transport, homes, cities and energy systems are not automatically bad. They can be expressions of our God-given calling to develop the potential of creation.

The question is whether our development serves life or damages it. Are we cultivating or consuming? Are we using creation gratefully or treating it as an infinite resource? Are we considering the vulnerable, the poor, future generations and non-human creatures, or only our immediate comfort?

A Christian approach should avoid two extremes. One extreme treats the environment as if it matters more than people. The other treats the environment as if it does not matter at all. The biblical picture does neither. Creation matters because God made it and loves it. People matter because they bear God’s image. Those two truths belong together.

If environmental policy harms the poor, ignores energy access or makes basic life unaffordable, Christians should care. But if cheap energy today damages the conditions of life for others tomorrow, Christians should care about that too. Love of neighbour is not limited to the neighbour I can see.

## Are Fossil Fuels Also Gifts from God?

This is where the conversation becomes more complicated. Fossil fuels are part of the created order. Coal, oil and gas did not appear outside God’s sovereignty. They have also brought real benefits. Modern medicine, transport, agriculture, heating, cooling, manufacturing and electricity have all depended heavily on fossil fuels. Many people are alive today because energy systems made food, medicine, refrigeration, clean water and transport more available.

So I do not think it is right to speak as though fossil fuels are simply evil. They are created resources that human beings have used, often with great benefit.

But “God provided it” does not automatically mean “we should keep using it in the same way forever.” God also made poisonous plants, uranium, iron ore, rivers, forests and human intelligence. The existence of a resource does not settle the ethics of how it should be used. The moral question is not only, “Did God make this?” but also, “What does wise and loving use look like now?”

Fossil fuels store energy from the past. When burned, they release greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. The scientific consensus is that human activities, especially greenhouse gas emissions, have caused global warming. That does not mean every weather event can be simplistically blamed on climate change, and it does not mean every proposed policy is wise. But it does mean Christians should take the basic problem seriously.

If a resource was useful for a time, but its continued large-scale use now creates serious harm, stewardship may mean transitioning away from it where we can. That is not ingratitude. It may be wisdom.

## What About Renewable Energy?

Renewable energy is also part of God’s provision. Sunlight, wind, water, geothermal heat and human creativity are gifts too. Solar panels and wind turbines are not “unnatural” simply because they involve technology. So do tractors, eyeglasses, wheelchairs, antibiotics and church sound systems.

The question is not whether renewable energy is perfectly clean. It is not. Solar panels, batteries, wind turbines and transmission infrastructure require mining, manufacturing, land use, labour, recycling and careful planning. Christians should not romanticise them.

But we should also not pretend that all energy sources have the same impact. If renewable energy can reduce pollution, lower greenhouse gas emissions and eventually provide reliable and affordable power, then it seems consistent with stewardship to support that transition. Not recklessly, not ideologically and not with indifference to cost or human impact, but seriously.

I think we should make the switch to renewable energy as far as we responsibly can. That does not mean flicking a switch overnight. It means planning, investing, improving storage and grids, supporting affected workers and communities, and being honest about trade-offs. It also means recognising that poorer nations may need different pathways from wealthy ones. Justice matters in both directions.

## Climate Change and Christian Responsibility

Climate change can be a frustrating topic because it is often discussed badly. Some people speak as though any concern about climate is panic or political manipulation. Others speak as though anyone who asks practical questions is morally corrupt. Christians should resist both instincts.

A Christian response should begin with truthfulness. If the best available evidence says human activity is warming the planet and increasing risks to ecosystems and communities, especially vulnerable ones, we should not dismiss that simply because the topic has become politically charged.

A Christian response should also be marked by humility. Most of us are not climate scientists, energy engineers or economists. We should be careful about making confident claims outside our expertise. But humility does not mean paralysis. I do not need to be a mechanic to know my car needs servicing when every qualified mechanic I trust tells me the engine is in trouble.

Climate change is not only an environmental issue. It is also a neighbour-love issue. The people most exposed to climate-related harm are often those with the least power and least historical responsibility. Drought, heat, food insecurity, rising seas, severe storms and displacement do not affect everyone equally. Christian love must be attentive to the vulnerable.

That does not mean every climate policy is automatically Christian. Some policies may be ineffective, unjust or naive. But doing nothing is also a policy. Inaction has consequences too.

## Stewardship Begins Close to Home

It is easy to talk about global systems and never change our own lives. It is also easy to obsess over personal choices while ignoring larger structures. Christian stewardship needs both personal faithfulness and public wisdom.

At a personal level, stewardship might include reducing waste, using energy wisely, supporting renewable options where possible, repairing rather than replacing, consuming less thoughtlessly, planting trees, caring for local places and choosing products with more attention to labour and environmental impact.

At a church level, stewardship might include energy audits, solar panels if suitable, less waste at events, thoughtful purchasing, better insulation, responsible use of church grounds and teaching that connects creation care with discipleship. Churches do not need to become environmental organisations, but they should not act as though creation care is irrelevant to worship and mission.

At a public level, Christians can support policies that encourage cleaner energy, protect vulnerable communities, improve public transport, reduce pollution, strengthen resilience and help workers transition from industries that may decline. That last point matters. If Christians advocate change while ignoring miners, power station workers and regional communities, we are not practising love well.

## Creation Care Is Not a Replacement Gospel

One concern I share is that environmental language can sometimes become a substitute gospel. The world’s deepest problem is not merely carbon emissions. Sin, idolatry, greed, injustice, alienation from God and death go deeper than any environmental crisis. The hope of the world is not finally human cleverness, political will or technology. It is Jesus Christ.

But that does not make creation care unimportant. The gospel is not less than personal salvation, but it is more than escape from the physical world. Jesus is Lord of all creation. The resurrection of Jesus is the beginning of new creation, not the abandonment of creation. Christian hope looks toward the renewal of heaven and earth, not the disposal of the earth as a failed experiment.

That means Christians should not say, “This world will burn anyway, so why care?” That is not biblical hope. It is closer to negligence dressed up as theology. If God intends to renew creation, then caring for creation now is not pointless. It is a sign of allegiance to the Creator and a foretaste of the world God will make right.

## So Where Do I Land?

I currently think Christians should support a serious transition toward renewable energy. Fossil fuels have served human life in many ways, and we should acknowledge that honestly. But gratitude for past usefulness does not require permanent dependence. If we have cleaner ways to power our homes, churches, transport and economies, then stewardship points us toward change.

That change should be wise, just and realistic. It should consider reliability, affordability, workers, poorer communities, energy access and the environmental costs of renewable technologies themselves. But the existence of trade-offs is not an excuse for inaction. Most serious moral decisions involve trade-offs.

For me, the basic Christian instinct should be this: God made the world, God loves the world, and God has placed human beings within it as responsible creatures. We are not called to worship creation, but neither are we free to trash it. We are called to love God, love our neighbours and care for the world that belongs to him.

Stewardship does not answer every technical question. It does not tell us the ideal energy mix for every country. It does not solve every policy debate. But it does give us a posture: gratitude instead of entitlement, restraint instead of greed, wisdom instead of denial, hope instead of despair, and love instead of indifference.

That seems like a good place for Christians to begin.
