Christianity began as a small Jewish movement centred on the claim that Jesus of Nazareth had been crucified, raised from the dead and revealed as Israel’s Messiah and the world’s Lord. Two thousand years later, Christian communities can be found across almost every nation and culture.

That long history includes courageous witness, theological insight, care for the poor, missionary sacrifice and movements for justice. It also includes division, political ambition, violence, colonialism, abuse and repeated failures to live according to Jesus’ teaching.

A brief history cannot tell every story. It will inevitably leave out important people, churches and movements. It can, however, provide a map. Understanding that map helps Christians recognise where their traditions came from, why churches differ and how earlier believers wrestled with questions that remain important today.

Church history should not be used to prove that one tradition has always been right. It should make us grateful, honest and humble.

## The First Christian Communities

Christianity emerged within first-century Judaism. Jesus and his earliest followers were Jewish. They worshipped the God of Israel, read Israel’s Scriptures and understood Jesus within the story of creation, covenant, exile and promised restoration.

After Jesus’ death and resurrection, his followers proclaimed that God had made him both Lord and Messiah. Acts describes the Holy Spirit empowering this witness at Pentecost and the message spreading from Jerusalem into Judea, Samaria and the wider Roman world.

The earliest Christians did not initially think of themselves as starting a separate religion. They believed that Israel’s hopes were being fulfilled through Jesus. As increasing numbers of non-Jews joined the movement, difficult questions emerged. Did Gentile believers need to become Jews? Must they be circumcised and keep the whole law of Moses?

Acts 15 describes an important gathering in Jerusalem where the apostles and elders concluded that Gentiles did not need to become Jewish proselytes to belong to the people of God. This decision helped Christianity grow as a multi-ethnic movement.

The apostles, especially Paul, established churches throughout the eastern Mediterranean. Christian communities met in homes, shared meals, baptised new believers, cared for vulnerable people and gathered around the teaching of the apostles. They faced internal disagreements, moral failures and external opposition from the beginning.

## Persecution, Witness and Growth

Roman persecution was not constant or identical throughout the empire. At times it was local and sporadic. At other times emperors authorised broader campaigns against Christians.

Christians could attract suspicion because they refused to worship the traditional gods or offer religious loyalty to the emperor. Their confession that “Jesus is Lord” challenged claims of ultimate imperial authority. Some were imprisoned or executed, and the stories of martyrs became powerful examples of faithfulness.

Yet Christianity continued to spread. It appealed across social classes and ethnic groups. Christians cared for the sick, rescued abandoned infants, supported widows and formed communities in which slaves and free people worshipped together, even though the church did not consistently challenge all the injustices of Roman society.

Early Christian thinkers also defended the faith intellectually. Writers such as Justin Martyr, Irenaeus, Tertullian and Origen responded to criticism, interpreted Scripture and clarified Christian belief. Their works show that the early church was never theologically uniform. Christians debated how to describe God, Christ, salvation, Scripture and the relationship between faith and philosophy.

## The Creeds and Councils

As Christianity spread, disagreements about Jesus became especially urgent.

Arius, a priest in Alexandria, taught that the Son was exalted above creation but was not eternally divine in the same sense as the Father. The Council of Nicaea in AD 325 rejected this teaching and confessed that the Son is of the same divine being as the Father.

The controversy continued for decades. The Council of Constantinople in AD 381 reaffirmed Nicene faith and gave fuller expression to belief in the Holy Spirit. The creed associated with these councils remains widely used among Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican and many Protestant churches.

Later debates concerned how Jesus’ divinity and humanity belong together. The Council of Chalcedon in AD 451 confessed Jesus Christ as one person in two natures, truly divine and truly human, without confusion or division.

These councils did not invent the divinity of Jesus or create the Trinity from nothing. They developed careful language to protect what Christians believed Scripture and worship already required. At the same time, politics, personalities and imperial power affected the debates. Some ancient churches rejected Chalcedon, contributing to divisions that remain today.

The canon of Scripture also developed over time. The core New Testament books were widely recognised early, while some books were disputed in certain regions. The process was not decided at Nicaea. Recognition emerged through use in worship, apostolic connection, theological coherence and widespread acceptance across the churches.

## From Persecuted Movement to Imperial Religion

A major change occurred under Emperor Constantine. After gaining power in the early fourth century, Constantine ended official persecution and favoured Christianity. Later in the century, Emperor Theodosius made Nicene Christianity the empire’s official religion.

This brought freedom, resources and public influence. Churches could build openly, bishops gained social authority and Christian teaching shaped law and culture.

It also created serious dangers. The church became closely connected with political power. People could identify as Christian for social advantage rather than conviction. Imperial rulers involved themselves in church disputes, and Christians sometimes used state power against religious opponents.

This uneasy relationship between church and political authority would shape much of later Christian history.

## Monasticism, Mission and the Medieval Church

Some Christians responded to the church’s increasing wealth and power by pursuing disciplined lives of prayer, simplicity and community. Monastic movements grew in the Egyptian desert and later across Europe.

Figures such as Anthony of Egypt, Basil of Caesarea and Benedict of Nursia shaped traditions of prayer, work, hospitality and shared life. Monasteries preserved manuscripts, educated clergy, cared for the poor and became centres of mission and learning. Monastic life could also accumulate wealth and require reform.

Christianity spread beyond the old Roman world. The church developed strongly in Ethiopia, Armenia, Persia, India and Central Asia as well as Europe. The Church of the East sent missionaries along trade routes as far as China. These histories are often overlooked when Christianity is told only as a European story.

In Western Europe, the bishop of Rome gradually gained influence and became known as the pope. In the East, major centres included Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch and Jerusalem. Differences in language, theology, worship and political authority widened over centuries.

The division between Eastern Orthodox and Western Catholic Christianity is commonly associated with the mutual excommunications of 1054, although the separation was gradual rather than a single event.

The medieval Western church produced significant thinkers such as Anselm, Thomas Aquinas and Julian of Norwich. Universities developed from cathedral and monastic schools. Churches created hospitals and systems of charity.

The same period included corruption, coercion and violence. The Crusades brought warfare carried out under Christian banners. Jews, Muslims, Eastern Christians and others suffered. Church offices could be bought or used for political advantage. Calls for reform arose repeatedly from within the church itself.

## The Protestant Reformation

By the early sixteenth century, dissatisfaction with abuses and demands for reform had become widespread.

In 1517 Martin Luther criticised the sale and preaching of indulgences. The dispute expanded into deeper questions about salvation, Scripture, church authority and the sacraments. Luther argued that sinners are justified by grace through faith and that Scripture is the final norm for Christian doctrine.

Other reform movements developed. Ulrich Zwingli and John Calvin led reforms in Switzerland. The English Reformation began partly through King Henry VIII’s break with papal authority and later developed distinct Protestant forms.

The Anabaptists called for believers’ baptism, voluntary church membership and a more radical separation between church and state. They were persecuted by both Catholic and Protestant authorities. Their emphasis on discipleship and religious liberty later influenced Baptist and other free-church traditions.

The Roman Catholic Church also pursued internal reform. The Council of Trent clarified Catholic doctrine, addressed abuses and strengthened the education and discipline of clergy. New communities such as the Jesuits contributed to education and global mission.

The Reformation made Scripture more accessible through translation and printing. It also produced lasting divisions, religious warfare and close alliances between churches and states. Protestants themselves divided over baptism, the Lord’s Supper, predestination, church government and worship.

## Revivals, Missions and New Movements

From the seventeenth century onward, new Protestant movements emerged.

Baptists emphasised believers’ baptism, local church life and liberty of conscience. Pietism encouraged personal devotion, Bible reading and practical faith. Methodism, associated with John and Charles Wesley, combined evangelical preaching with disciplined small groups, social concern and an emphasis on holy living.

The evangelical revivals of the eighteenth century brought widespread preaching and conversion in Britain and North America. They also encouraged missionary societies, Bible distribution and campaigns for social reform.

Modern missionary movements carried Christianity into new regions. Missionaries translated Scripture, established schools, founded hospitals and planted churches. Some defended indigenous peoples and opposed exploitation.

Mission was also frequently entangled with colonial expansion. Western Christians sometimes treated their own culture as superior, ignored existing Christian communities or failed to challenge imperial injustice. The history of mission therefore includes both costly service and serious wrongdoing.

Local Christians were never merely passive recipients. African, Asian, Pacific and Latin American believers interpreted, taught and spread the faith in their own contexts. In many places, Christianity grew most strongly through local evangelists and churches rather than foreign missionaries.

## Christianity in the Modern World

The nineteenth and twentieth centuries brought industrialisation, scientific development, democratic reform, global war and changing approaches to biblical scholarship.

Christians responded in different ways. Some withdrew from modern culture. Others embraced it too quickly. Many tried to engage new knowledge while holding to historic faith.

Movements for abolition, education, worker protection, civil rights and humanitarian care often drew on Christian convictions. Christians were also involved in slavery, segregation, nationalism and colonial oppression. Church history rarely allows a simple division between heroes and villains.

Pentecostalism emerged in the early twentieth century with a strong emphasis on the Holy Spirit, spiritual gifts, healing and evangelism. It grew rapidly across the world and now includes many distinct traditions.

The ecumenical movement sought greater cooperation among divided churches. The Second Vatican Council renewed many areas of Roman Catholic life and encouraged deeper engagement with Scripture, other Christians and the modern world.

Christianity’s demographic centre has increasingly shifted away from Europe and North America. Churches in Africa, Asia, Latin America and the Pacific have become central to global Christianity. This challenges Western Christians to recognise that their experience is not the whole church.

## What Church History Teaches Us

Church history does not provide a perfect age to which Christians can simply return.

The early church was close to the apostles, but it still experienced conflict and confusion. The councils clarified central doctrines, but they were affected by political power. The medieval church preserved learning and cared for communities, but it also accumulated wealth and used coercion. The Reformers recovered important truths, but they divided, persecuted and sometimes relied on state violence. Missionaries served sacrificially, but mission could become entangled with colonialism.

History warns Christians against romanticising their own tradition.

It also reminds us that the church is larger than our denomination, nation or century. We inherit Scripture, creeds, hymns, theological insights and practices from believers we may never have encountered.

Studying history can deepen gratitude without requiring uncritical admiration. It can expose recurring temptations such as political control, celebrity, tribalism and the protection of institutions at the expense of people. It can also introduce us to courage, repentance, creativity and faithfulness in difficult circumstances.

The history of Christianity is ultimately not a record of flawless Christians preserving a flawless institution. It is the story of imperfect people bearing witness to Jesus, sometimes faithfully and sometimes disastrously.

That should lead neither to cynicism nor triumphalism. It should lead to humility.

Christians today are adding another chapter. The question is not merely whether future generations will remember our churches, leaders or arguments. It is whether they will see communities that learned from the past, told the truth about their failures and lived more faithfully under the lordship of Christ.
