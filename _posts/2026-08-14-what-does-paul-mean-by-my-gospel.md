Sometimes Christians speak about “Paul’s gospel” as if Paul had a separate message from Jesus, Peter, James or the rest of the New Testament. That can be confusing. I thought there was only one gospel. So why does Paul speak in Romans 2:16, Romans 16:25 and 2 Timothy 2:8 about “my gospel”? Was Paul claiming ownership of a different message? Did he preach a gospel of grace while Jesus preached a gospel of the kingdom? Did the later church merge together voices that were never really saying the same thing?

Those questions matter because the gospel is not a small topic. If there are competing gospels in the New Testament, then Christian faith becomes unstable. If Jesus preached one message and Paul preached another, then we have to decide which one to follow. But if Paul’s gospel is the same gospel of Jesus Christ, entrusted to Paul for a particular mission, then the phrase “my gospel” is not a problem. It becomes a window into Paul’s calling, emphasis and apostolic responsibility.

My basic conclusion is this: Paul does not have a different gospel. He has a particular commission to proclaim the one gospel, especially among the Gentiles.

## Why Paul Says “My Gospel”

Paul uses the phrase “my gospel” in a few places. In Romans 2:16, he speaks of the day when God judges people’s secrets “according to my gospel.” In Romans 16:25, he says God is able to strengthen believers “according to my gospel and the preaching of Jesus Christ.” In 2 Timothy 2:8, he says, “Remember Jesus Christ, risen from the dead, descended from David, as preached in my gospel.”

At first glance, “my gospel” sounds possessive. But Paul does not mean he invented it. In Galatians 1, he insists that the gospel he preached did not originate from human beings. He received it through a revelation of Jesus Christ. That does not mean Paul had no connection with the other apostles. It means his authority as an apostle did not depend on borrowing their message second-hand. He was not a freelancer creating his own religious system. He was a servant entrusted with a message.

The phrase “my gospel” is probably best understood in the same way we might say “my ministry,” “my calling” or “my message this Sunday.” It does not mean we own the content absolutely. It means we are responsible for faithfully proclaiming what has been entrusted to us.

Paul makes this clear elsewhere. In 1 Corinthians 15, he reminds the church of the gospel he preached, which they received and in which they stand. He then summarises the message: Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, was buried, was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures and appeared to witnesses. Paul says this was not his private invention. It was what he had received and passed on.

So when Paul says “my gospel,” he is not saying, “This is my alternative version.” He is saying, “This is the gospel entrusted to me, preached by me and central to my apostolic mission.”

## There Is Only One Gospel

Paul is fierce about this in Galatians. He does not say there are multiple valid gospels depending on personality, culture or apostolic brand. He says that if anyone preaches a gospel contrary to the one the Galatians received, that person is under judgment. That is strong language, but it shows how seriously Paul took the unity and truth of the gospel.

This is important because Paul was not relaxed about gospel confusion. He could be flexible about customs, food, travel plans and ministry strategy. But he was not flexible about the message of Christ.

The gospel, for Paul, is centred on Jesus the Messiah, crucified and risen, through whom God fulfils his promises, defeats sin and death, justifies sinners, creates a new people and launches new creation. This is not a different gospel from Jesus’ proclamation of the kingdom. It is the announcement that the kingdom has come through Jesus’ death, resurrection and lordship.

Sometimes Christians contrast Jesus and Paul too sharply. Jesus, they say, preached the kingdom of God, while Paul preached justification by faith. There is a real difference in emphasis, but not a contradiction. Jesus announced God’s reign arriving in and through himself. Paul announced that Jesus, the crucified and risen Messiah, is Lord, and that through him Jews and Gentiles alike are brought into God’s renewed people by grace.

The language differs because the mission context differs. Jesus preached within Israel before the cross and resurrection. Paul preached after the resurrection, as the gospel went out among the nations. But the centre is the same: God’s saving reign has come in Jesus Christ.

## Paul’s Particular Calling to the Gentiles

One reason Paul can call it “my gospel” is that he was given a particular responsibility for the Gentiles. In Galatians 2, Paul says that the gospel for the uncircumcised was entrusted to him, just as the gospel for the circumcised was entrusted to Peter. That does not mean two different gospels. It means one gospel entrusted to different apostles for different mission fields.

Paul’s calling was especially concerned with showing that Gentiles did not need to become Jews in order to belong to the people of God. They did not need circumcision or Torah boundary markers to be full members of God’s family. They needed Christ.

That is why justification by faith is so central in Paul’s letters. It is not an abstract theological hobby. It is bound up with the question of who belongs at the same table. If Gentiles are justified by faith in Christ, then they are not second-tier believers. They are not guests hovering near the edge of Israel’s promises. They are full participants in the people of God.

This is also why the conflict in Galatians is so intense. Paul is not merely upset about a minor ritual. He sees that adding circumcision as a requirement for Gentile believers undermines the truth of the gospel. It says, in effect, that Christ is not enough to mark out the people of God.

Paul’s gospel, then, has a distinctive Gentile mission emphasis. But it remains the one gospel of Jesus Christ.

## Paul and the Other Apostles

Paul was not isolated from the rest of the early church. Galatians 2 describes Paul going to Jerusalem and setting before the recognised leaders the gospel he preached among the Gentiles. The result was not a rival church or a divided message. James, Cephas and John recognised the grace given to Paul and gave him and Barnabas the right hand of fellowship.

That matters. Paul wanted to make clear that his gospel did not come from human approval, but he also wanted to show that his gospel was not out of step with the apostolic witness. The apostles did not need to correct Paul’s message. They recognised his calling.

There was still conflict. Paul confronted Peter at Antioch because Peter’s behaviour compromised table fellowship with Gentile believers. But notice the nature of the conflict. Paul did not say Peter preached a different Jesus. He said Peter’s behaviour was “not in step with the truth of the gospel.” In other words, the problem was inconsistency between the gospel they shared and the practice Peter temporarily adopted.

This is a helpful distinction. Christians can confess the same gospel and still fail to live out its implications. Paul’s confrontation of Peter was not proof of two gospels. It was proof that the gospel has social consequences.

If Jews and Gentiles are one in Christ, then the church cannot rebuild the walls Christ has broken down. If sinners are justified by grace, then no group can claim spiritual superiority. If Jesus is Lord, then ethnicity, status and religious achievement cannot be the basis of belonging.

## Did Paul Change Jesus’ Message?

A common claim is that Jesus preached a simple message about love, mercy and the kingdom, but Paul turned Christianity into a complicated religion about sin, justification and doctrine. That claim sounds plausible only if we flatten both Jesus and Paul.

Jesus certainly preached the kingdom of God. But Jesus also spoke about sin, judgment, repentance, forgiveness, discipleship, his own authority and the significance of his death. He did not merely offer general moral wisdom. He announced that God’s reign was arriving through his own person and mission.

Paul certainly taught doctrine. But his theology was not detached from life. His letters call churches to love, holiness, unity, generosity, forgiveness, endurance, prayer and mission. Paul does not replace Jesus’ call to discipleship. He applies it in light of the cross, resurrection and gift of the Spirit.

The difference is partly historical. Jesus ministered before Good Friday and Easter. Paul preached after them. Jesus moved toward the cross. Paul preached from the reality that the cross and resurrection had happened. It would be strange if Paul did not speak explicitly about what Jesus’ death and resurrection accomplished.

Paul does not change Jesus’ gospel. He proclaims the crucified and risen Jesus as the fulfilment and climax of that gospel.

## What Paul Emphasises

Paul’s letters highlight several themes with particular force.

First, Paul emphasises grace. Salvation is not earned by human achievement, ethnic privilege, law observance or moral superiority. It is God’s gift in Christ.

Second, Paul emphasises union with Christ. Believers do not merely admire Jesus from a distance. They are in Christ. They share in his death and resurrection. Their identity is reshaped around him.

Third, Paul emphasises justification by faith. God declares sinners to be in the right through faith in Christ, not through works of the law. This is both deeply personal and deeply communal.

Fourth, Paul emphasises the inclusion of the Gentiles. The gospel creates one new people, not two separate classes of believers.

Fifth, Paul emphasises life in the Spirit. The Christian life is not merely rule-keeping. It is Spirit-empowered transformation.

Sixth, Paul emphasises the lordship of Jesus. The risen Jesus is not only a private saviour. He is Lord over all things.

None of these themes compete with Jesus. They unfold the meaning of Jesus’ life, death, resurrection and reign.

## What Goes Wrong When We Separate Paul from Jesus

When Christians separate Paul from Jesus, several problems follow.

One problem is that we can end up with a reduced Jesus. Jesus becomes only an ethical teacher, stripped of his cross, resurrection and lordship. That is not the Jesus of the Gospels.

Another problem is that we can end up with a reduced Paul. Paul becomes a cold theologian who cared only about doctrines and arguments. That is not the Paul who wept, prayed, suffered, loved churches and urged believers to walk in humility, patience and love.

A third problem is that we can divide what the New Testament holds together. Kingdom and cross belong together. Grace and obedience belong together. Justification and discipleship belong together. Personal salvation and the creation of a new people belong together.

There are also more extreme versions of this problem. Some groups have tried to build a Christianity almost entirely around Paul, treating Jesus’ earthly teaching as if it belonged to a different dispensation or was mainly for Israel. Others reject Paul as if he distorted Jesus’ message. Both moves are dangerous. The New Testament gives us one apostolic witness to one Lord.

Paul must be read with Jesus, and Jesus must be read in light of the whole biblical story that reaches its climax in his death and resurrection.

## So What Is Paul’s Gospel?

If we had to summarise Paul’s gospel, we might say something like this:

God has acted in Jesus the Messiah to fulfil his promises, deal with sin, defeat death, include the nations, create a new people and launch new creation. Jesus died for our sins, was raised from the dead, is Lord of all and will judge and renew the world. People are brought into this saving reality by grace through faith, not by ethnic status, law observance or human achievement. Those who belong to Christ receive the Spirit and are called to live as God’s renewed people.

That is not a different gospel from Jesus. It is the gospel of Jesus proclaimed after the cross and resurrection, with particular clarity for the mission to the nations.

Paul’s phrase “my gospel” should not make us nervous. It should remind us that the gospel is both received and entrusted. Paul received it from Christ, preached it faithfully and guarded it fiercely. He knew it was not his property, but he also knew it had become his responsibility.

The same is true for the church today. The gospel is not ours to reinvent. It is ours to receive, believe, live, guard and proclaim.

## Conclusion

There is only one gospel. Paul did not invent a second one. He was entrusted with the gospel of Jesus Christ and sent especially to proclaim it among the Gentiles.

His language, emphasis and mission context sometimes differ from the Gospels, but the centre is the same. Jesus is the crucified and risen Messiah. God’s promises are fulfilled in him. Sinners are reconciled to God through him. Jews and Gentiles are brought into one family through him. The Spirit forms a new people in him. Creation’s future is secured in him.

So when Paul says “my gospel,” we should not hear rivalry. We should hear stewardship. Paul is saying, in effect, “This is the gospel entrusted to me. This is the message I have been called to preach. This is the truth I will suffer for, guard and pass on.”

That is not a different gospel. It is the one gospel, personally entrusted and faithfully proclaimed.
