Some Christians can recite the Apostles' Creed or Nicene Creed from memory. Others belong to churches where creeds are never read aloud, confessions are rarely mentioned and the closest thing to a creed is the church's "Statement of Faith" on the website. That can create an uncomfortable question. Am I missing something important if I belong to churches that affirm the basic Christian faith, but do not make regular use of historic creeds or confessions?

I have mostly belonged to Baptist and broadly evangelical churches. These churches usually do not speak about "the creeds" or "the confessions" in the same way many Anglican, Lutheran, Presbyterian, Reformed, Catholic or Orthodox churches do. Instead, they usually have a statement of faith. It may affirm the Trinity, the person and work of Jesus, the authority of Scripture, salvation by grace through faith, the work of the Holy Spirit, the church and the return of Christ. In practice, that statement may do some of the same work as a creed or confession, even if it uses a different name.

So what are creeds and confessions? Why do some churches use them heavily while others are cautious? Which ones define Christianity itself? Is a statement of faith the same thing? And should Christians in non-creedal churches feel like they are missing out?

## Why This Question Matters

This question matters because Christianity is not whatever an individual Christian happens to feel is true. The faith was received, confessed, taught and defended before any of us arrived. The New Testament itself speaks of teaching being handed on, guarded and confessed.

At the same time, Christians rightly insist that Scripture is the final authority. Creeds and confessions can help the church, but they are not Scripture. When used well, they serve Scripture. When used badly, they can become tribal badges, barriers to correction or substitutes for careful Bible reading.

So the issue is not whether we must choose between Scripture and creeds. The better question is how summaries of faith can help the church remain faithful to Scripture.

## What Is a Creed?

The word "creed" comes from the Latin *credo*, meaning "I believe." A creed is a concise summary of Christian belief, often designed for public confession, teaching, baptism, worship or boundary-setting.

Creeds are not meant to replace Scripture. At their best, they summarise what Christians believe Scripture teaches on central matters. They are like a map. A map is not the land itself, but a good map can help travellers understand where they are, where danger lies and how major landmarks relate to each other.

The two most widely recognised Christian creeds are the Apostles' Creed and the Nicene Creed. The Apostles' Creed developed as a baptismal confession in the Western church. The Nicene Creed, more precisely the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed, emerged from the fourth-century debates about the identity of Jesus and the Holy Spirit. It confesses the full divinity of the Son and the Holy Spirit and remains central in Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, Lutheran and many other traditions.

These creeds do not answer every theological question. They say nothing explicit about baptismal mode, church government, Calvinism, Arminianism, the millennium, spiritual gifts or the exact nature of hell. Their purpose is more basic. They identify the central shape of Christian faith: one God, Father, Son and Holy Spirit, creation, incarnation, crucifixion, resurrection, ascension, judgment, forgiveness, the church and resurrection hope.

## What Is a Confession?

A confession is usually longer and more detailed than a creed. It sets out what a particular church tradition believes about a wider range of doctrines. Where a creed might define basic Christianity, a confession often defines a theological tradition.

Examples include the Augsburg Confession in Lutheranism, the Westminster Confession of Faith in Presbyterian and Reformed churches, the Thirty-Nine Articles in Anglicanism, the Second London Baptist Confession and various modern Baptist statements of faith. These documents address matters such as Scripture, salvation, sacraments or ordinances, church government, covenant, law, worship, civil authority and final judgment.

Confessions often arise in moments of controversy, reform or identity formation. They answer the question, "What do we mean when we say we are this kind of Christian?" They can be deeply helpful because they clarify what a church teaches and prevent every generation from having to reinvent doctrine from scratch.

Yet confessions can also be misused. If treated as untouchable or almost equal to Scripture, they can become a barrier to correction. If used as weapons, they can create unnecessary suspicion or tribalism. A healthy confession should serve the church's faithfulness to Scripture, not replace Scripture's authority.

## What Is a Statement of Faith?

A statement of faith is a summary of what a church, school, ministry or organisation believes. It is often shorter than a historic confession and may be written in modern language for a particular context.

For many Baptist and evangelical churches, the statement of faith functions as the local doctrinal summary. It tells members, leaders and visitors what the church believes about Scripture, God, Jesus, salvation, the Spirit, the church, baptism, communion and last things. It may also clarify positions on marriage, ministry leadership or ethical issues.

A statement of faith can be very helpful. It is accessible. It can be written for a local church's needs. It may avoid unnecessary detail on secondary issues. It can allow Christians who agree on the gospel to serve together even when they differ on less central matters.

But a statement of faith can also be thin. If it is too brief, vague or detached from historic Christianity, it may not give enough guidance. It may assume everyone means the same thing by words like "God," "Jesus," "gospel" or "Bible" when that is not always true.

## Are Statements of Faith the Same Thing?

A statement of faith is not exactly the same as a creed or confession, but it belongs to the same family. All three are summaries of belief. All three try to say, "This is what we believe."

The difference is usually scope, history and function. A creed is short, ancient and focused on central Christian identity. A confession is longer, more detailed and often tied to a tradition. A statement of faith is usually modern, local or organisational, and often designed to be clear for members, visitors or ministry partners.

So when a church says, "We do not use creeds, we just have a statement of faith," it may still be doing a creedal thing without using the word. The church is still summarising doctrine, drawing boundaries and teaching what it believes.

That is not necessarily a problem. The danger is when churches imagine they have no tradition and no creed at all. In reality, every church has some doctrinal framework. The question is whether it is clear, biblical, historically aware and honest.

## The Core Creeds That Define Historic Christianity

When people ask which creeds all Christians follow, the answer needs care. Not all Christians formally use the same documents in worship. Some traditions recite them weekly. Others affirm their teaching without naming them often.

The Apostles' Creed and Nicene Creed are the most important basic summaries in Western and global Christian history. The Nicene Creed is especially significant because it defines orthodox Christian belief about the Trinity and the full divinity of Christ. A church that denies the Nicene faith is not merely disagreeing about a secondary issue. It is moving outside historic Christianity.

The Chalcedonian Definition is also important because it clarifies that Jesus Christ is one person in two natures, fully God and fully human. Many ordinary Christians have never heard of Chalcedon, but its teaching protects the biblical confession of who Jesus is.

In that sense, the doctrines behind these creeds define Christianity: the Trinity, the incarnation, the death and resurrection of Jesus, forgiveness of sins, the church, judgment and resurrection hope. Christians may differ on many things, but these are not optional extras.

## Why Some Churches Use Detailed Confessions

Some churches use detailed confessions because they value doctrinal clarity. Confessions help teach the faith, guard against error and unite churches around shared convictions. They can be especially useful for training leaders, resolving disputes and preserving theological memory.

Detailed confessions also remind us that we are not the first Christians to read the Bible. The church has wrestled with Scripture for centuries. Creeds and confessions connect us with that wider conversation.

This can be a gift. In a culture that prizes individual opinion, confessions remind us that Christian faith is received before it is personalised. They slow us down. They force us to ask whether our preferred interpretation is faithful to the church's historic witness.

## Why Some Churches Avoid Them

Other churches avoid creeds and confessions because they fear anything that seems to compete with Scripture. Some Christians have inherited slogans like "no creed but the Bible." The instinct behind that slogan is understandable. Scripture is uniquely inspired and authoritative. No human summary should be placed above it.

Free church traditions also value local church autonomy and believer responsibility. They may worry that detailed confessions can bind consciences too tightly, exclude genuine believers over secondary matters or preserve old disputes that no longer serve the church well.

There is wisdom in some of this caution. Creeds and confessions can be misused. But rejecting them entirely can create other problems. A church without written doctrinal clarity may still have unwritten rules, hidden assumptions or vague teaching. Sometimes "no creed but the Bible" really means "no creed but our interpretation of the Bible, left unstated."

## Am I Missing Out?

If you belong to a church that affirms the basic Christian faith through a statement of faith rather than historic creeds or detailed confessions, you are not necessarily missing the gospel. A church can be faithful, biblical and Christ-centred without reciting the Nicene Creed every Sunday.

However, you may be missing some of the richness of the wider Christian tradition if the historic creeds are never taught, explained or acknowledged. The creeds can help Christians see that their faith is not a recent invention. They guard us from vague views of God and Jesus. They connect us with believers across centuries, cultures and denominations.

So my answer is both reassuring and challenging. No, you are not automatically missing out in a spiritually dangerous way if your church uses a statement of faith rather than a formal confession. But yes, you may be missing a helpful resource if the great creeds are ignored entirely.

For Baptist and evangelical churches, the best path may not be to become something else. It may be to remain committed to Scripture while becoming more historically aware. A church can have a statement of faith and still teach the Apostles' Creed, explain the Nicene Creed and show how these summaries protect biblical truth.

## Conclusion: Roots Without Replacing Scripture

Creeds, confessions and statements of faith are not Scripture. They should never replace the Bible or silence correction from the Word of God. But when used well, they can help the church confess, teach and guard the faith.

A creed summarises the central Christian faith. A confession explains the convictions of a tradition in more detail. A statement of faith states what a church or ministry believes, often in a local and accessible form. They are not identical, but they are related.

Christians in churches with simple statements of faith should not feel inferior to Christians in more confessionally formal traditions. But neither should we ignore the riches of the historic church. The goal is not to collect documents for their own sake. The goal is to confess the truth faithfully.

A good statement of faith needs roots. The historic creeds can help provide them. They remind us that Christianity is not whatever we decide it should be. It is the faith once delivered to the saints, centred on the Triune God and the crucified and risen Lord Jesus Christ.
