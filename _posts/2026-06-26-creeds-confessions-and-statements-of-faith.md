Some Christians can recite the Apostles’ Creed or Nicene Creed from memory. Others belong to churches where creeds are never read aloud, confessions are rarely mentioned and the closest thing to a creed is the church’s “Statement of Faith” on the website. That can create an uncomfortable question. Am I missing something important if I belong to churches that affirm the basic Christian faith, but do not make regular use of historic creeds or confessions?

I have mostly belonged to Baptist and broadly evangelical churches. These churches usually do not speak about “the creeds” or “the confessions” in the same way many Anglican, Lutheran, Presbyterian, Reformed, Catholic or Orthodox churches do. Instead, they usually have a statement of faith. It may affirm the Trinity, the person and work of Jesus, the authority of Scripture, salvation by grace through faith, the work of the Holy Spirit, the church and the return of Christ. In practice, that statement may do some of the same work as a creed or confession, even if it uses a different name.

So what are creeds and confessions? Why do some churches use them heavily while others are cautious? Which ones define Christianity itself? And should Christians in non-creedal churches feel like they are missing out?

## What Is a Creed?

The word “creed” comes from the Latin *credo*, meaning “I believe.” A creed is a concise summary of Christian belief, often designed for public confession, teaching, baptism, worship or boundary-setting.

Creeds are not meant to replace Scripture. At their best, they summarise what Christians believe Scripture teaches on central matters. They are like a map. A map is not the land itself, but a good map can help travellers understand where they are, where danger lies and how major landmarks relate to each other.

The two most widely recognised Christian creeds are the Apostles’ Creed and the Nicene Creed. The Apostles’ Creed developed as a baptismal confession in the Western church. The Nicene Creed, more precisely the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed, emerged from the fourth-century debates about the identity of Jesus and the Holy Spirit. It confesses the full divinity of the Son and the Holy Spirit and remains central in Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, Lutheran and many other traditions.

These creeds do not answer every theological question. They say nothing explicit about baptismal mode, church government, Calvinism, Arminianism, the millennium, spiritual gifts or the exact nature of hell. Their purpose is more basic. They identify the central shape of Christian faith: one God, Father, Son and Holy Spirit, creation, incarnation, crucifixion, resurrection, ascension, judgment, forgiveness, the church and resurrection hope.

## What Is a Confession?

A confession is usually longer and more detailed than a creed. It sets out what a particular church tradition believes about a wider range of doctrines. Where a creed might define basic Christianity, a confession often defines a theological tradition.

Examples include the Augsburg Confession in Lutheranism, the Westminster Confession of Faith in Presbyterian and Reformed churches, the Thirty-Nine Articles in Anglicanism, the Second London Baptist Confession and various modern Baptist statements of faith. These documents address matters such as Scripture, salvation, sacraments or ordinances, church government, covenant, law, worship, civil authority and final judgment.

Confessions often arise in moments of controversy, reform or identity formation. They answer the question, “What do we mean when we say we are this kind of Christian?” They can be deeply helpful because they clarify what a church teaches and prevent every generation from having to reinvent doctrine from scratch.

Yet confessions can also be misused. If treated as untouchable or almost equal to Scripture, they can become a barrier to correction. If used as weapons, they can create unnecessary suspicion or tribalism. A healthy confession should serve the church’s faithfulness to Scripture, not replace Scripture’s authority.

## What Is a Statement of Faith?

A statement of faith is the more common term in many evangelical, Baptist, Pentecostal and independent churches. It is usually a summary of the church’s core beliefs, often written in modern language and shaped by the needs of that local church, denomination, school or ministry.

So is a statement of faith the same thing as a creed or confession?

The answer is: partly.

A statement of faith is creed-like because it summarises what the church believes. It is confession-like because it identifies a church’s doctrinal commitments. But it is often less historically rooted, less liturgical and less detailed than older creeds and confessions.

In practice, many churches that say “we have no creed but the Bible” still have some kind of creed. They may not call it that, but as soon as they say, “This is what we believe the Bible teaches,” they are making a doctrinal summary. That is not necessarily a bad thing. In fact, it is unavoidable. The real question is whether the summary is faithful, clear, humble and accountable to the wider Christian tradition.

## Why Do Some Churches Use Creeds and Confessions?

Some churches use creeds and confessions because they see themselves as part of a long, historic stream of Christian worship and teaching. Reciting the Nicene Creed is not merely an intellectual exercise. It joins present-day believers with Christians across centuries and cultures. It reminds the church that Christianity did not begin with our local congregation, favourite preacher, Bible college or preferred denomination.

Creeds also teach. Many Christians learn theology through repeated worship before they learn it through books. Saying “true God from true God” or confessing Jesus as “begotten, not made” teaches people that Jesus is not a creature, not a lesser divine being and not merely a moral example.

Confessions also protect churches from vagueness. A church might say it believes the Bible, but so do many groups that disagree sharply about what the Bible teaches. A confession clarifies what the church means.

For pastors and teachers, confessions can also provide guardrails. They do not solve every issue, but they help keep the church centred on established Christian teaching rather than the personality or preferences of whoever is currently leading.

## Why Do Some Churches Avoid Them?

Other churches avoid creeds and confessions because they fear elevating human documents above Scripture. This concern is not silly. Jesus criticised religious leaders who allowed human traditions to distort God’s commands. The Reformers themselves insisted that church teaching must remain under Scripture.

Many Baptist, restorationist and independent evangelical churches have been especially cautious here. They often want to preserve liberty of conscience, congregational responsibility and direct submission to Scripture. In some cases, the phrase “no creed but the Bible” expresses a sincere desire not to bind believers beyond what Scripture teaches.

There is something valuable in that instinct. Christians should not treat later summaries as though they carry the same authority as Scripture. A creed or confession can be wrong. It can be incomplete. It can be overextended. It can reflect the blind spots of its time.

However, “no creed but the Bible” can also be misleading. Everyone interprets the Bible. Everyone summarises doctrine. Everyone has some framework for deciding what Christianity is and is not. A church that rejects historic creeds may still have unwritten assumptions that function like a creed, but without the same clarity or accountability.

## Which Creeds Define Christianity?

Not every creed or confession defines Christianity itself. Some define particular traditions within Christianity. The Westminster Confession, for example, is important in Reformed theology, but someone does not have to affirm every part of Westminster to be a Christian. The same is true of Baptist, Anglican or Lutheran confessions.

The ecumenical creeds are different. “Ecumenical” here does not mean vague or lowest-common-denominator. It means widely received across the major historic streams of Christianity. The most important are the Apostles’ Creed and Nicene Creed. Some traditions also give special attention to the Athanasian Creed, especially for its careful statements about the Trinity and the incarnation.

The Nicene Creed is especially important because it protects the church’s confession of who God is and who Jesus is. Christianity is not merely belief in God, belief in morality or admiration for Jesus. Historic Christianity confesses the one God as Father, Son and Holy Spirit, and confesses Jesus Christ as truly God and truly human.

A church may not recite the Nicene Creed every week, or ever, but if it denies the substance of Nicene faith, it has moved outside historic Christianity. That is why beliefs about the Trinity and the person of Christ are not secondary details. They are foundational.

## Am I Missing Out?

So am I missing out if I belong to churches that affirm basic Christian doctrine but do not regularly use creeds or confessions?

In one sense, yes, there may be something missing. Historic creeds can give Christians a stronger sense of continuity with the wider church. They can deepen worship, sharpen doctrine and protect us from thinking Christianity began with our own local context. They can also help us see that some doctrines are not optional extras, but part of the basic grammar of Christian faith.

There is something spiritually healthy about saying, “I believe,” with the church across time and space. It reminds us that faith is personal but not private. We are not lone interpreters inventing Christianity for ourselves.

But in another sense, no, belonging to a church that uses a statement of faith rather than historic creeds does not mean you are necessarily being deprived of real Christianity. If the church faithfully teaches Scripture, confesses the Trinity, proclaims Christ crucified and risen, calls people to repentance and faith, practises baptism and communion, and seeks to live as the people of God, then it is not failing simply because it does not use the word “creed.”

The issue is not whether a church uses old language or new language. The issue is whether it clearly confesses the apostolic faith.

## Statements of Faith Can Be Helpful, But They Need Roots

A modern statement of faith can be useful. It can speak clearly into a local church’s context. It can address issues that older creeds did not directly address. It can help newcomers understand what a church teaches.

But a statement of faith is stronger when it is consciously rooted in the historic Christian faith. A local church should not need to choose between Scripture and tradition, as though the two are enemies. Scripture is the final authority, but the church has been reading Scripture for two thousand years. Wisdom means listening carefully to that long conversation, even while testing all things by Scripture.

For churches like mine, which tend to use statements of faith rather than formal confessions, the challenge is not necessarily to become more liturgical or confessional in style. The challenge is to be historically aware. We can ask: Does our statement of faith reflect the central convictions of the wider Christian church? Do our people understand the Trinity? Do they know why Jesus must be truly God and truly human? Do they understand resurrection hope? Do they know which beliefs are essential and which are secondary?

If the answer is no, then perhaps the church does not need a new label. It needs better teaching.

## Why This Matters

Creeds and confessions matter because doctrine matters. Not doctrine as a cold intellectual hobby, but doctrine as the church’s attempt to speak truthfully about God, salvation and life in Christ.

A creed cannot save us. A confession cannot replace faith. A statement of faith cannot make a church healthy by itself. But careful summaries of Christian belief can serve the church by helping us remember what we have received, recognise error, teach the next generation and worship the God who has made himself known.

I do not think Christians in non-creedal churches need to feel inferior. But I do think we benefit from knowing the creeds, even if our churches do not regularly recite them. They help us locate ourselves within the wider body of Christ.

A good statement of faith may be enough for a local church’s practical needs. But it should never make us forget that we belong to a much bigger story. Before our denomination, before our local church and before our own theological preferences, Christians have confessed: Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father.
