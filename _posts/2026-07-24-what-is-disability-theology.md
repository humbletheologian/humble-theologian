Disability theology asks what happens when disability is not treated as a side issue, a pastoral problem or a tragic exception to normal life, but as a serious place from which to think about God, humanity, church and hope.

That does not mean disability replaces Scripture as our authority. It means the lived experience of disabled people exposes questions that Christians should have been asking all along. What does it mean to be made in the image of God? What kind of body did the risen Jesus have? How should churches understand healing? Who gets treated as valuable? Who is missing from the table? What do our buildings, services, sermons and assumptions say about the gospel we claim to believe?

At its best, disability theology is not merely theology about disabled people. It is theology with disabled people, from disabled people and for the whole church. It reminds us that disability is not an awkward appendix to Christian doctrine. It touches creation, incarnation, resurrection, community, worship, discipleship and the hope of new creation.

## Disability Theology Is Not the Same as Pastoral Sensitivity

Many churches think about disability mainly in terms of care. Someone needs a ramp, a lift, transport, prayer, pastoral support or help getting to a seat. These things matter. Practical access is not beneath theology. A church that preaches welcome while remaining unnecessarily inaccessible has a theology problem, not merely a facilities problem.

But disability theology goes deeper than kindness or accommodation. It asks how Christian doctrine itself has been shaped by assumptions about normality, strength, intelligence, independence and productivity.

For example, Christians often say that every person is made in the image of God. But if our worship, leadership structures and ministry expectations quietly assume that the ideal Christian is able-bodied, articulate, independent, emotionally regulated, socially confident and endlessly available, then our practice is contradicting our doctrine.

Disability theology asks whether we have confused cultural ideals of ability with Christian maturity.

It also challenges some harmful patterns in Christian teaching. Disability has sometimes been treated as punishment for sin, a test of faith, an object lesson for others or a problem that exists mainly so God can display miraculous power. Scripture is more complex than that. Jesus rejects simplistic links between disability and personal sin in John 9. Paul speaks of weakness as a place where Christ’s power is made known, but not in a way that turns suffering into a spectacle. The risen Christ still bears wounds. The Christian story does not fit neatly into either despair or triumphalism.

## Major Voices in Disability Theology

One of the most important names in modern disability theology is Nancy Eiesland. Her book *The Disabled God* is often treated as a landmark text. Eiesland argued that the risen Christ who appears to the disciples still bearing the wounds of crucifixion gives Christians a powerful image of God’s solidarity with disabled people. The disabled body is not simply erased by resurrection. The wounds of Jesus are not hidden as shameful defects. They become part of how his identity is recognised.

Eiesland’s work was especially significant because it drew from disability rights movements and pushed back against the idea that disabled people are merely objects of charity. She helped place disabled people within theology as agents, interpreters and witnesses.

Amos Yong has made a major contribution through his work on disability, the Holy Spirit, Pentecost and Down syndrome. Yong’s work is especially important because he brings disability into systematic theology rather than leaving it only in pastoral care. He asks what disability means for theological anthropology, the church and the Spirit’s work among diverse bodies and minds.

John Swinton has written extensively on disability, mental health, dementia, time, personhood and pastoral care. His work is especially helpful because it refuses to reduce people to cognition, usefulness or memory. He has helped many Christians think more carefully about what it means to be known by God and held within community when a person does not fit modern ideals of speed, independence or productivity.

Thomas Reynolds’ *Vulnerable Communion* has also shaped the field. Reynolds argues that vulnerability is not a defect to be overcome but part of creaturely life. His work helps the church resist the fantasy that human beings are meant to be self-sufficient. We are created for communion, dependence and mutual receiving.

Deborah Beth Creamer is another important voice. She has challenged narrow definitions of disability and explored how limits, bodies and variation shape theological reflection. Her work helps move the discussion beyond a simple disabled/non-disabled divide and asks how all human beings live with limits, dependence and embodiment.

Jennie Weiss Block, Brian Brock, Hans Reinders, Bethany McKinney Fox and Sarah Jean Barton have also made significant contributions, especially around friendship, practical theology, intellectual disability, healing, church practice and embodied discipleship. The field is now wide enough that no single thinker defines it.

Still, Eiesland remains especially important for physically disabled Christians because her work began so explicitly with disabled embodiment, access, justice and the wounded body of Christ.

## What Disability Theology Has Given the Church

Disability theology has given the church several important gifts.

First, it has challenged the church’s assumptions about the image of God. If the image of God is tied to rationality, speech, independence or moral agency in a narrow way, then many disabled people are treated as lesser image-bearers. Disability theology pushes Christians to ground human dignity in God’s creative and covenantal action, not in measurable capacity.

Second, it has exposed the difference between inclusion and belonging. Inclusion often means, “You may come into the space we designed without you in mind.” Belonging asks a deeper question: “How must the community change because you are truly part of us?”

Third, it has challenged shallow approaches to healing. Christians should be free to pray for healing. The Gospels show Jesus healing bodies, restoring people to community and confronting the social exclusion attached to illness and disability. But disabled people should not be treated as failed healing projects. Nor should they be pressured to perform gratitude, inspiration or spiritual heroism for others.

Fourth, disability theology has reminded the church that weakness is not the opposite of Christian faithfulness. The gospel does not glorify worldly strength. Jesus comes in humility, suffers in weakness and rises with wounds. Paul does not boast in impressive power but in weakness through which Christ is known.

Fifth, it has made practical access a theological issue. Ramps, hearing loops, captions, clear communication, accessible bathrooms, transport, seating, sensory awareness and flexible participation are not merely administrative details. They reveal what a church believes about the body of Christ.

## Where the Field Has Often Focused

In recent years, much disability theology has focused on intellectual disability, dementia, mental health and neurodivergence. There are good reasons for this. These areas press deeply on questions of personhood, memory, cognition, communication, agency and community. They challenge churches that have often equated faith with verbal expression, intellectual comprehension or conventional behaviour.

This work is necessary. The church needs it. People with intellectual disabilities, dementia, mental illness and neurodivergent experiences have often been marginalised, misunderstood or spoken about without being heard. Theological work in these areas has helped expose some of the most damaging assumptions in Christian communities.

But there is a danger if the field unintentionally swings so far in these directions that physical disability becomes less visible. That would be ironic, because much early disability theology, including Eiesland’s work, was deeply concerned with physical access, bodily difference, social barriers and the politics of space.

Many physically disabled Christians still experience church as a place designed for other bodies. Stages are inaccessible. Communion is served in ways that assume mobility. Prayer ministry assumes standing. Small groups meet in inaccessible homes. Ministry opportunities assume stamina, transport and physical presence. Leadership pipelines assume the body can keep pace with the institution. Even when the theology is welcoming, the architecture and expectations may say otherwise.

Physical disability also raises theological questions that deserve more sustained attention. What does discipleship look like with chronic pain, fatigue, mobility limits or dependence on equipment and carers? How should churches think about embodiment when some bodies are marked by scars, braces, wheelchairs, continence needs, spasms, weakness or medical routines? How do we talk about resurrection hope without implying that disabled bodies are merely embarrassing temporary failures? How do we honour the desire for healing without suggesting that a physically disabled life is only a waiting room for a better one?

These questions are not peripheral. They belong at the centre of Christian theology because Christianity is an embodied faith.

## The Neglected Place of Physical Disability

As someone with a physical disability, I find myself grateful for the growth of disability theology, while also wondering whether physically disabled people are sometimes assumed rather than actually engaged.

There is a difference between being mentioned and being heard.

Physical disability often appears in church conversations as an access issue. Can the person get into the building? Is there a ramp? Can they reach the bathroom? These questions matter, but they are only the beginning. Physical disability also affects time, energy, employment, social life, ministry, intimacy, public visibility, dependence, pain and dignity.

A wheelchair user, amputee, person with cerebral palsy, person with spina bifida, person with muscular dystrophy, person with chronic illness or person with limited mobility may not face the same questions as someone with dementia or an intellectual disability. There is overlap, but not sameness.

Physical disability often makes the body publicly visible. People stare, avoid, over-help, under-help, speak to companions instead of the disabled person or assume incompetence. In Christian spaces, people may also spiritualise the body. They may ask intrusive questions, offer unsolicited healing prayer, interpret disability as a testimony-in-waiting or assume the disabled person must have unusually deep faith because of suffering.

There is room for a richer theology of visible bodily difference. The church needs language for disabled bodies that are neither hidden nor turned into inspirational symbols. We need a theology that can speak of ramps and resurrection, continence and communion, fatigue and faithfulness, pain and participation, scars and sacraments.

## Gaps Still Needing Attention

Several gaps remain in disability theology.

First, we need more work from physically disabled theologians and pastors, not only about them. Lived experience does not automatically make someone right, but it does help expose questions others may not know to ask. The church needs disabled scholars, preachers, pastors, chaplains and ministry leaders shaping the conversation.

Second, we need more attention to ordinary church practice. Disability theology can become abstract if it does not ask what happens on Sunday morning, in small groups, at leadership meetings, in baptism services, during communion, at camps and in pastoral care systems.

Third, we need better theology of access. Access is often treated as compliance or kindness. It should be understood as a matter of ecclesiology. If the church is the body of Christ, then excluding disabled bodies is not simply unfortunate. It is a failure to discern the body.

Fourth, we need more theological reflection on fatigue, pain and limited capacity. Churches often reward the people who can attend everything, volunteer endlessly and respond quickly. But faithfulness may look different when energy is limited. A theology of vocation must make room for bodies that cannot be endlessly productive.

Fifth, we need more work on disability and leadership. Churches may welcome disabled people as recipients of care but hesitate to recognise them as leaders. Physical disability can be treated as a logistical inconvenience rather than a source of wisdom. Yet disabled leaders may see aspects of dependence, community, patience and perseverance that the wider church urgently needs.

Sixth, we need more reflection on healing that listens to disabled people. The question is not whether God can heal. The question is how the church can pray for healing without making disabled people feel spiritually defective, socially awkward or pastorally managed.

Seventh, we need more attention to class, cost and geography. Disability is expensive. Equipment, transport, housing, medical care and support services all shape participation in church and society. Rural, regional and lower-income disabled people may face barriers that theology written from comfortable academic settings does not see.

Eighth, we need an Australian contribution. Much disability theology comes from North America and the United Kingdom. Australian churches have their own contexts: the NDIS, wide geography, smaller congregations, volunteer-heavy ministries, ageing buildings and a public culture that can be both egalitarian and dismissive. Australian disability theology should speak from these realities.

## What Might Still Be Contributed

There is plenty still to be done.

One contribution would be a theology of physical access as discipleship. Accessibility should not be reduced to a building checklist. It is a way of learning to love actual neighbours rather than imaginary average people.

Another contribution would be a theology of disabled leadership. The church needs to ask not only, “How can we include disabled people?” but, “How might disabled people help lead us into a more faithful understanding of the gospel?”

A third contribution would be a theology of embodied inconvenience. Much exclusion happens not because churches hate disabled people but because disabled bodies interrupt efficiency. They require planning, patience, adaptation and sometimes money. But Christianity is built around inconvenient love. The Good Samaritan was interrupted. Jesus stopped for people others passed by. The body of Christ is not meant to be efficient at the expense of love.

A fourth contribution would be a theology of healing and honour. Churches need a way to pray boldly while honouring disabled people fully in the present. A disabled Christian should not have to choose between believing God can heal and believing their life now has dignity.

A fifth contribution would be a theology of resurrection that does not despise the present body. Christian hope includes bodily renewal, but that hope should not make disabled people feel that their current bodies are meaningless mistakes. The risen Jesus is transformed, but his wounds are not erased from recognition. That should make Christians careful about how we speak.

A sixth contribution would be practical resources for churches. The field needs books and articles, but local churches also need checklists, Bible studies, sermons, training material, pastoral guides and examples of accessible ministry done well.

## Why This Matters for the Whole Church

Disability theology is sometimes treated as a niche concern. It is not. It is a test of whether the church believes its own gospel.

If salvation is by grace, why do we value people according to usefulness? If the church is one body, why are some bodies treated as optional? If Christ was crucified and raised with wounds, why are we embarrassed by weakness? If the Spirit gives gifts to the whole church, why do we assume leadership must look able-bodied? If the kingdom belongs to the poor, weak and overlooked, why do our churches so often mirror the values of strength, speed and success?

Disability theology does not ask the church to become less biblical. It asks the church to become more biblical. It asks us to read Scripture with people who have too often been present in the room but absent from the interpretation.

For physically disabled Christians, the next stage of the conversation may require saying, gently but clearly, that we are not only symbols of access, suffering or inspiration. We are theologians, disciples, leaders, servants, friends and members of Christ’s body.

The church does not need disability theology because disabled people are special projects. The church needs disability theology because without disabled people, its understanding of God, humanity, weakness, healing, community and hope becomes too small.
