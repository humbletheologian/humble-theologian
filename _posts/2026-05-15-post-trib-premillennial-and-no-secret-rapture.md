End-times theology can become strange very quickly. Charts multiply. Timelines become rigid. Current events get matched to biblical symbols. Fear replaces hope. Curiosity replaces discipleship. The return of Christ becomes less a promise that strengthens the church and more a code to be cracked.

I do not want that kind of eschatology. I believe the return of Christ matters deeply, but I do not think it should make Christians anxious, arrogant or speculative. Biblical hope should produce endurance, holiness, mission, courage and worship.

At present, I lean **post-tribulational**, **premillennial** and **no secret rapture**. In simple terms, I believe Christ will return publicly and gloriously after tribulation, raise and gather his people, defeat evil and reign in victory. I do not believe Scripture requires a two-stage return where believers are secretly removed before a final public coming years later.

I hold this view with humility. Faithful Christians have disagreed about the millennium, tribulation and rapture for a long time. This is not a test of fellowship. It is an attempt to make sense of Scripture while keeping the focus where Scripture keeps it: Christ will return, evil will be judged, the dead will be raised and God will make all things new.

## What the View Means

The three parts of the view belong together.

**Post-tribulational** means I do not expect the church to be removed from the earth before a final period of tribulation. The New Testament repeatedly prepares believers for endurance, witness and faithfulness under pressure. Jesus tells his disciples to expect suffering. Paul encourages churches to persevere through hardship. Revelation calls the saints to patient endurance.

**Premillennial** means I lean toward the view that Christ returns before the millennium described in Revelation 20. I recognise that amillennial and postmillennial readings are held by serious Christians. Revelation is symbolic, pastoral and apocalyptic, so I do not want to pretend the passage is simple. Even so, I currently think some form of future reign of Christ and the saints before final judgment makes best sense of the passage.

**No secret rapture** means I do believe believers will be caught up to meet the Lord, but I do not think that event is a hidden evacuation of the church years before Christ’s public appearing. The issue is not whether believers meet Christ. The issue is how that meeting relates to his return.

## Why I Struggle with the Secret Rapture

The popular rapture view many Christians know is the idea that believers will suddenly disappear before a final period of tribulation. The world then continues through a distinct end-times crisis before Christ returns publicly later. I understand why people find that comforting. It seems to promise escape before the worst suffering comes.

But I do not find it the clearest reading of the New Testament.

The passages often used to support the rapture, especially 1 Thessalonians 4, speak of believers meeting the Lord as he comes. The picture is not necessarily of the church being taken away from earth for years. It can be understood as the people of the king going out to meet him and welcome him as he arrives. The direction of hope is Christ’s appearing, resurrection and reunion, not escape from creation.

I also struggle with dividing Christ’s return into multiple stages. The New Testament speaks of the coming, appearing, revelation and day of the Lord in rich and varied ways, but the overall picture seems to be one climactic public event. Christ is revealed. The dead are raised. Evil is judged. The people of God are gathered. Creation is brought toward renewal.

That does not sound secret. It sounds cosmic.

## Why Post-Tribulation Makes Sense to Me

A post-tribulation view seems to fit the New Testament’s call to endurance. The church is not told to build its hope on avoiding suffering. It is told to remain faithful in suffering.

This does not mean Christians should seek hardship or become obsessed with persecution. Nor does it mean God abandons his people. Scripture often shows God preserving his people through trial rather than removing them from it. Israel passes through the sea. Daniel is preserved in the lions’ den. The three friends are preserved in the fire. The early church bears witness under pressure.

The book of Revelation fits this pattern. It does not encourage the church to decode headlines so it can know when to escape. It calls the saints to worship the Lamb, resist idolatry, endure pressure and bear faithful witness.

This also matters pastorally. Many Christians around the world already experience persecution, poverty, war and instability. A theology built around guaranteed escape can sound strange to believers whose discipleship has always involved suffering. The New Testament hope is stronger than escape: Christ is faithful, resurrection is coming and evil will not have the final word.

## Why I Remain Premillennial

I lean premillennial because Revelation 20 appears to describe a reign of Christ and the saints connected with resurrection, vindication and the defeat of evil before the final judgment and new creation.

I know this is debated. Amillennial Christians often argue that the thousand years symbolises Christ’s present reign or the heavenly reign of the saints. Postmillennial Christians tend to emphasise the gospel’s transforming effect in history before Christ’s return. These views are not foolish. They are serious attempts to read Scripture faithfully.

Still, some form of future millennial reign currently seems persuasive to me. It gives space for Christ’s victory to be displayed publicly in history before the final state. It also resonates with Old Testament hopes of justice, peace and the nations coming under the rule of God.

That said, I do not want my premillennialism to become sensational. Revelation was written to form faithful witnesses, not anxious codebreakers. If premillennialism makes someone obsessed with speculation, it has gone wrong. If it strengthens hope, endurance and worship, it is bearing better fruit.

## How This Connects with New Creation

My rejection of a secret rapture connects with my belief in new creation. The Christian hope is not evacuation from earth. It is the return of Christ, the resurrection of the dead, the judgment of evil and the renewal of creation.

That matters for discipleship now. If the world is simply a doomed stage from which believers will soon be removed, creation care, justice, peacemaking, neighbour love and long-term discipleship can seem less important. That is not always what rapture teachers intend, and many Christians who believe in a pre-tribulation rapture are deeply faithful. But rapture culture can sometimes feed escapism.

The hope of new creation pushes in a different direction. We do not save the world by our own power, but we do bear witness to the coming kingdom. We live now as people whose Lord will return publicly and whose future is resurrection life in a renewed world.

## What This Means for Discipleship

End-times theology should shape the kind of people we become. The practical question is not merely, “Can I draw the perfect timeline?” It is, “Am I ready to follow Christ faithfully until he comes?”

A healthy eschatology should produce watchfulness without panic. It should produce courage without arrogance. It should produce holiness without withdrawal. It should produce mission without triumphalism. It should make us more patient, prayerful and grounded in the hope of Christ’s return.

This is why I am cautious about rapture-focused teaching that encourages Christians to read every crisis as proof that escape is near. The New Testament expectation is stronger and more demanding. Christ will return. The dead will be raised. Evil will be judged. God will make all things new. Until then, the church witnesses, suffers, serves and hopes.

## What I Still Hold Loosely

I still wrestle with apocalyptic symbolism. Revelation is not an easy book. I do not want my confidence to outrun the clarity of Scripture.

I also know that sincere Christians read the millennium differently. Some see the thousand years as symbolic of Christ’s present reign. Some expect a future earthly reign. Some see the tribulation as a specific final period. Others see tribulation as the church’s ongoing experience between Christ’s first and second comings.

Because of that, I want to hold this view with humility. I can learn from amillennial Christians who emphasise the present reign of Christ. I can learn from historic premillennial Christians who emphasise endurance. I can even learn from dispensational Christians when they take prophecy, Israel and future hope seriously. My concern is not to win an end-times chart.

My concern is to keep the return of Christ connected to faithful discipleship.

## Where I Currently Stand

For now, I lean post-tribulational, premillennial and no secret rapture. I expect one glorious return of Christ, the resurrection and gathering of his people, the judgment of evil and the eventual renewal of all creation. I believe the church should expect endurance rather than guaranteed escape from tribulation. I lean toward a future millennial reign, though I hold the details with humility.

This view helps me keep end-times teaching connected to hope rather than fear. The return of Jesus is not a puzzle for the curious few. It is the promise that the crucified and risen Lord will finish what he began.

The question is not only, “What will happen?”

The question is, “What kind of people should we be?”

Faithful. Patient. Watchful. Courageous. Hopeful. Ready for the King.
