Christianity is never lived in a cultural vacuum. The gospel is not owned by any nation, class or political tribe. Yet every Christian community lives the faith through particular languages, histories, habits, assumptions and fears. That means Christianity can look quite different in Western developed countries, even where churches share similar doctrines.

I notice this especially when comparing Australia and the United States. Both are Western, English-speaking, historically shaped by Christianity and deeply influenced by individualism, consumerism and secular modernity. But the way Christianity functions culturally can feel very different. This matters because Christians can confuse their cultural expression of faith with the faith itself.

I want to be fair, but I do not want to be so balanced that the article says nothing. There are faithful, humble Christians in both Australia and America. There are churches in both places that love Jesus, serve the poor, teach Scripture carefully and bear costly witness. But the cultural temptations are not equal in shape. In my view, the more dangerous public temptation in American Christianity is the confusion of the kingdom of God with national power. In Australia, the danger is almost the opposite: a polite, private Christianity that becomes too embarrassed to speak clearly. Both need correction, but I am more cautious about importing American religious confidence than Australian reserve.

## The gospel is not national property

The New Testament does not present Christianity as the possession of one nation. Jesus announces the kingdom of God, not the greatness of Rome, Israel, Australia or America. The church is a people gathered from every tribe, language, people and nation. That makes Christian faith local in expression, but never national in ownership.

This is why cultural discernment matters. A church in Perth should not pretend it has no cultural location. It should speak to local people in recognisable language, with pastoral sensitivity to local assumptions and wounds. But local expression becomes dangerous when it stops being expression and becomes captivity. The church should be recognisably local, but not captive to the local gods.

Every culture receives the gospel as both gift and judgment. The gospel affirms what is good, exposes what is idolatrous and calls people into a new kingdom. That is true for Australia. It is true for America. It is true for every Western society that has learned to baptise comfort, autonomy, consumer choice and political identity.

## The American temptation: greatness, visibility and power

From the outside, American Christianity can seem closely tied to national identity. Christian language appears in politics, civic ceremonies, public debates and national self-understanding. Many Americans are used to hearing their country described in religious terms. That visibility can create opportunities. Christian vocabulary is not foreign. Churches can be large and influential. Faith can be publicly discussed without immediate embarrassment.

I do not want to dismiss the gifts that have come from American Christianity. Many of the books, sermons, podcasts, seminaries and theological resources that have helped Christians around the world have come from the United States. American churches have often been generous, mission-minded, innovative and serious about Christian education. Much of what has helped me has come from American Christians.

But visibility is not the same as faithfulness. When Christianity becomes closely tied to national greatness, political identity or cultural superiority, it risks losing the humility of Jesus. The cross can be replaced by the flag. Faithfulness can be measured by political loyalty. The kingdom of God can be confused with national power. The gospel does not teach any nation to boast that it is the greatest. It calls the church to witness to a crucified and risen Lord.

This is where I want to be clear. Christians should reject Christian nationalism. Not because Christians should be silent in public life, but because the church’s allegiance to Christ must not be fused with the destiny, mythology or power of any nation-state. The church can pray for a nation, serve a nation, critique a nation and bless neighbours within a nation. But it must not identify the nation with the kingdom of God.

American Christianity can also be shaped by individualism, consumerism and political polarisation. Those are not uniquely American problems, but they can be intensified in a culture that prizes freedom, success and influence. The result can be a Christianity that speaks strongly about rights but less strongly about service; strongly about victory but less strongly about weakness; strongly about being right but less strongly about being humble.

## The Australian temptation: quiet, private and embarrassed

Australia has different temptations. Public Christianity is often more subdued. Australians can be suspicious of religious intensity, institutional authority and confident claims. We have a strong cultural instinct against self-importance. That can be frustrating for Christians who want faith to be taken seriously. But it can also be a gift. Australian culture can expose religious performance and puncture spiritual ego.

In Australia, Christianity often needs to be lived before it is listened to. Trust matters. Humility matters. Pastoral care matters. Grand claims may be ignored unless they are embodied in patient, ordinary faithfulness. That fits the theme of Humble Theologian well: learning, loving and living the faith.

At the same time, Australian reserve can drift into apathy. Christians may become so cautious about sounding pushy that they say very little. We may hide conviction under humour or understatement. We may confuse humility with silence. Churches can become comfortable, polite and low-expectation. The danger is not triumphalism, but quiet accommodation.

So I do not think the Australian church should simply congratulate itself for being less loud than America. A quiet church is not automatically a faithful church. Jesus calls disciples to witness. The gospel is public truth, not merely private comfort. If American Christianity can be tempted by power, Australian Christianity can be tempted by invisibility.

## Consumer Christianity in the West

Both countries share the problem of consumerism. Churches can become products. Worship can become preference. Discipleship can become self-improvement. People choose churches like they choose service providers: music style, preaching style, children’s programs, convenience and social fit.

Some of those things matter. Churches should care about quality, safety, accessibility and pastoral responsibility. A church that ignores children, disability, abuse prevention or basic hospitality should not hide behind anti-consumer language. But consumer Christianity makes the self the centre. It asks, “Does this church meet my needs?” before asking, “How is Christ forming me to love God and neighbour?”

This is a Western problem, not merely an American or Australian one. The market trains us to think as consumers. The gospel retrains us to live as disciples. Churches should be welcoming, but they are not religious service providers. They are communities being formed by Word, Spirit, worship, service, repentance and love.

## Individualism and the body of Christ

Western cultures also prize individual autonomy. That affects how we read the Bible, make decisions and understand church. We can treat faith as a personal project: my beliefs, my growth, my calling, my quiet time, my preferences.

Christian faith is personal, but not private or isolated. We belong to a body. Baptism joins us to a people. The Lord’s Supper is a communal meal. Spiritual gifts are given for the building up of others. Pastoral care, accountability and service all require community.

Australia’s laid-back individualism and America’s assertive individualism may look different, but both can resist the deeper belonging of the church. In both cultures, Christians need to recover the idea that discipleship is not simply the private improvement of religious individuals. It is life together under the lordship of Christ.

## What Australian Christians should and should not import

Because so much Christian media comes from the United States, Australian Christians need to be careful about what we import. We should gladly receive good teaching wherever it comes from. We should learn from American theological seriousness, evangelistic confidence, generosity, creativity and public courage. I have personally benefited from American pastors, scholars, apologists and churches.

But we should not import every American battle. Some controversies assume a cultural and political situation that is not ours. Some rhetoric relies on American constitutional language, party politics, culture-war instincts or national mythology. When Australian Christians adopt those debates uncritically, we can sound like we are speaking with someone else’s accent, fighting someone else’s war and missing our own mission field.

Australia needs public Christian witness, but not imported outrage. We need conviction, but not performance. We need courage, but not culture-war addiction. We need to speak about Jesus in ways that are serious, local, humble and clear.

## What America and Australia can teach each other

American Christianity, at its best, can teach Australians boldness, generosity, theological seriousness and confidence in public witness. Australian Christianity, at its best, can teach Americans restraint, suspicion of hype, humour about ourselves and resistance to religious showmanship.

But if I have to choose which correction Australian Christians most need, I would say this: we should learn from American confidence without copying American captivity to power. The answer to Australian timidity is not nationalism, celebrity Christianity or political aggression. The answer is Spirit-filled, Scripture-shaped, locally embodied witness.

Australians may need to be less embarrassed by conviction. Americans may need to be more suspicious of power. Australians may need to speak more clearly. Americans may need to listen more carefully. Both need to be discipled by Jesus more than by national habits.

## Why this matters for theology

Theology is never done from nowhere. A Christian in Perth, Sydney, Dallas or London may read the same Bible, but cultural assumptions shape which questions feel urgent. Americans may wrestle more visibly with church and state, religious liberty and political identity. Australians may wrestle more with secular indifference, institutional mistrust and how to speak about faith without sounding imported or artificial.

Bible college students need to notice this. When we read American books, listen to American podcasts or import American church debates, we should ask what translates and what does not. Some resources are excellent. Others assume a cultural situation that is not ours. The same is true in reverse. Australian caution is not automatically biblical wisdom.

Pastoral care also changes with culture. Pastoral care in Perth may not look exactly like pastoral care in Texas, London or Nairobi. Church life is culturally embedded. Theological conclusions may be shared across cultures, but their expression requires wisdom.

## Where I currently stand

Christianity does not belong to America, Australia or the West. It belongs to Christ. Every culture receives the gospel as both gift and judgment. The task is not to copy another nation’s expression of faith. The task is to follow Jesus here, among these people, with these challenges and opportunities.

For Western Christians, the challenge is to recognise how much our discipleship has been shaped by comfort, autonomy, politics and consumerism. The answer is not to despise our cultures, but to let Christ re-form us within them.

So here is where I land. I am grateful for much American Christian teaching, but I am wary of American-style Christian nationalism, celebrity, outrage and power-language. I am grateful for Australian humility and suspicion of hype, but I am wary of Australian silence, cynicism and low expectations. I want an Australian Christianity that is neither embarrassed nor aggressive: serious but not performative, convictional but not triumphalistic, publicly thoughtful but pastorally grounded.

The church should be recognisably local, but not captive to the local gods. We need Christians who can think deeply without becoming arrogant, love generously without becoming vague and live faithfully without confusing the kingdom of God with any national culture.
