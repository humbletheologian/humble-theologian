Artificial intelligence has moved quickly from novelty to normality. It can draft, summarise, search, compare, translate, brainstorm and organise information with remarkable speed. For Christians who study, preach, teach, write or compile resources, AI can feel like a gift. It can also feel unsettling. Is it a helpful assistant or a shortcut that weakens thinking? Can it support Bible study or distort it? Is it useful for sermon preparation or spiritually dangerous? What about academic integrity? And how does AI relate to tools such as Logos Bible Software?

My basic view is simple: AI can be a useful tool, but it must never become the teacher, authority or conscience of the Christian. It can help us gather, organise and test ideas, but it cannot replace Scripture, wisdom, prayer, community, pastoral discernment or the slow work of formation.

## AI Is a Tool, Not a Source of Wisdom

The first thing to say is that AI is not wisdom. It can produce confident sentences without truly understanding God, people, suffering, holiness or love. It can imitate theological language without spiritual maturity. It can sound pastoral without actually knowing the person being cared for. It can summarise arguments without reverence for Scripture.

That does not make it useless. A library catalogue is not wisdom either, but it can help you find books. A concordance is not wisdom, but it can help you trace words. Bible software is not wisdom, but it can help you study. AI belongs in that category. It is a tool that may assist human work.

The danger comes when we treat the tool as an authority. Christians should not ask AI to decide what a passage means and then accept the answer uncritically. We should not use it to avoid reading. We should not let it replace careful exegesis, prayerful reflection, trusted teachers or the hard work of thinking.

## What AI Does Well

AI is especially good at first-draft support. It can help brainstorm article outlines, discussion questions, sermon angles, study pathways, reading plans or possible objections. It can help organise scattered notes into a clearer structure. It can rewrite clunky sentences, simplify complex paragraphs or suggest headings.

It can also compare broad positions. If used carefully, it can help a student see the difference between Calvinism, Arminianism, provisionism and Molinism, or between annihilationism, eternal conscious torment and universalism. It can explain unfamiliar terms, suggest further reading and identify areas that need checking.

For ministry, AI can help compile resources. It can turn a sermon theme into possible group questions. It can create a first version of a prayer roster, reading guide, devotional outline or pastoral care checklist. It can help leaders who are time-poor get from a blank page to a workable draft.

That is a real benefit. Many Christians are not trying to be lazy. They are tired, under-resourced and trying to serve faithfully. Used well, AI can reduce friction and free people to spend more time on prayer, people and discernment.

## What AI Does Badly

AI can be wrong. It can invent facts, misstate sources, flatten nuance and present speculation as certainty. It may give a plausible answer to a biblical question while missing context, genre, historical background or theological tension. It may blend traditions without noticing that they disagree.

AI can also be biased toward what sounds balanced rather than what is true. It may overstate minority views to appear fair or understate serious doctrinal differences to sound polite. It can produce a generic evangelical answer when a Baptist, Anglican, Catholic or Orthodox answer would differ.

It can also encourage shallow learning. If a student asks AI for an answer before wrestling with the text, the hard work of formation is bypassed. Struggle is not always a problem. Sometimes the process of reading, comparing, doubting, checking and revising is where growth happens.

The biggest danger is not that AI will always be obviously wrong. The bigger danger is that it will often be almost right. Almost right is harder to detect.

## Integrity, Truth and Responsibility

For Christians, the ethical question is not simply, "Can I get away with using AI?" The question is whether our use of AI is truthful, responsible and faithful.

In academic work, students must follow their institution's AI policy. If a lecturer says AI may be used for brainstorming but not drafting, then using it to draft would be dishonest. If AI use must be disclosed, then it should be disclosed. Submitting AI-generated work as if it were one's own is not merely a technical breach. It is a failure of integrity.

There is also a formation issue. The purpose of theological education is not only to produce assignments. It is to form judgment, habits of reading and theological responsibility. If AI does the thinking for us, we may receive a grade while becoming less capable as students of Scripture.

Truth also matters in church contexts. A preacher should not quote a source that has not been checked. A Bible study leader should not pass on a claim about Greek, Hebrew or history simply because AI said it. A resource compiler should not invent references or attribute words to people who did not say them.

## AI in Research and Study

AI can be helpful at the beginning of research. It can suggest search terms, summarise a debate, list major views, generate questions to ask of a text or identify possible sources. It can help a student understand the landscape before reading more deeply.

But AI should not be the final source. Research should move from AI assistance to verifiable sources: books, peer-reviewed articles, primary texts, official documents and trusted scholars. If AI says that a scholar argues something, check the scholar. If AI gives a quotation, verify it. If AI summarises a theological position, compare it with the best representatives of that position.

A useful pattern is: use AI to orient, then read real sources, then use AI again to test clarity. For example, a student might ask AI to explain the major views on hell, then read Stott, Fudge, Packer, Talbott or other relevant writers, then ask AI to help compare the arguments. The human student remains responsible for judgment.

## AI in Academic Work

Academic use is one of the most sensitive areas. AI can help with grammar, structure, outlining, brainstorming and identifying possible gaps. It can make writing clearer, especially for students who struggle to organise thoughts.

But it can also cross the line into doing the work. If AI writes the argument, selects the evidence and produces the prose, the student is no longer demonstrating their own learning. Even when the final result sounds polished, it may not represent the student's understanding.

For theological students, this matters spiritually. Academic honesty is not a secular rule added to Christian study. It is part of discipleship. We are called to love God with our minds, not outsource our minds while keeping the appearance of competence.

Good use of AI in academia should be transparent, limited and subordinate to learning. Bad use hides, replaces thinking and treats assessment as an obstacle rather than formation.

## AI in Sermon Preparation

AI can help preachers brainstorm illustrations, possible sermon structures, discussion questions or ways to explain a difficult concept. It can help identify where a sermon draft is unclear, too long or too abstract. It can suggest pastoral applications that the preacher may then weigh carefully.

But AI should not write the sermon in place of the preacher. Sermons are not theological essays delivered aloud. They are acts of pastoral proclamation to a particular people in a particular place. A preacher must wrestle with the text, pray, listen to the congregation's needs and speak as someone under the Word.

There is also a danger of generic sermons. AI can produce smooth, safe, predictable material. It may sound biblical without being deeply rooted in the passage. It may provide applications that are true in general but not piercing in context.

The preacher can use AI like a research assistant or editor, but not as the preacher. The sermon must come through prayer, study, conviction and pastoral love.

## AI in Bible Studies and Christian Resources

AI can be very useful for Bible studies. It can suggest observation, interpretation and application questions. It can create leader notes, summaries, opening questions and prayer prompts. It can help adapt material for seasoned Christians, newer Christians, youth, mixed groups or people with limited time.

It can also help compile Christian resources. For a website, church group or ministry, AI can sort topics, propose reading pathways, draft blurbs, create indexes and identify gaps. It can make resource development faster and more consistent.

The risk is that questions become too leading, too shallow or too detached from the text. A Bible study should help people read Scripture, not merely discuss religious ideas. AI-generated questions need to be checked against the passage. Are they observing what is actually there? Are they pushing a predetermined answer? Are they pastorally sensitive? Are they accessible without becoming simplistic?

## AI in Logos Bible Software

Logos Bible Software is an interesting case because its AI tools can be tied to a user's library. Features such as Smart Search, Smart Bible Search, summarisation and study assistance are designed to help users find and process material from identifiable resources. That is a significant advantage over asking a general chatbot for theological claims without sources.

If Logos points back to books, commentaries and articles in a user's library, the user can check the source. That makes AI more accountable. It can help locate relevant material faster, summarise long sections and surface connections that might otherwise be missed.

But even here, discernment is needed. A summary is not a substitute for reading the argument. A search result is not a conclusion. A commentary is not Scripture. Logos can make study more efficient, but efficiency is not the same as maturity.

The best use of AI in Logos is to move more quickly toward real sources, not away from them.

## Practical Guidelines for Christian Use

A few guidelines can help.

Use AI openly. Do not hide it where disclosure is required or where honesty would expect it.

Use AI early, not finally. Let it help with orientation, brainstorming and structure, but do not let it have the last word.

Check sources. Verify quotations, claims, dates, statistics and theological summaries.

Stay responsible. If you preach it, teach it, submit it or publish it, you are responsible for it.

Protect pastoral care. Do not put private or sensitive information into tools that should not receive it.

Keep reading. AI should not replace Scripture, books, commentaries, prayer or conversation with mature believers.

Let AI serve love. If it helps you communicate more clearly, prepare more faithfully or support people better, it may be a gift. If it makes you lazy, dishonest, dependent or shallow, it has become a problem.

## Conclusion: Useful, But Not Ultimate

Christians do not need to fear every new technology. Nor should we baptise every tool simply because it is useful. AI is powerful, but it is not wise. It can help with study, research, sermons, Bible studies, resources and software workflows. It can save time and improve clarity. It can also mislead, flatten, fabricate and tempt us toward dishonesty.

The right posture is neither panic nor naive enthusiasm. It is disciplined discernment.

AI can be a servant. It should not be the teacher. It can help us organise thoughts, but it cannot replace the mind renewed by Scripture. It can assist ministry, but it cannot replace pastoral love. It can summarise books, but it cannot produce wisdom. It can help us study the Bible, but it must never become the voice we trust more than the Word of God.
