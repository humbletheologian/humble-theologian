Artificial intelligence is no longer something Christians can treat as a distant technological issue. It is already shaping how people study, write, search, teach, preach, create resources and make decisions. Some Christians are excited by it. Others are suspicious of it. Both reactions are understandable.

AI can feel like a gift. It can summarise difficult material, suggest questions, compare views, organise research, draft outlines and help people who struggle with writing get started. For someone studying theology, preparing a Bible study or compiling resources for church, it can save hours. It can also feel unsettling. It can produce confident mistakes, flatten complex arguments, hide where ideas came from, encourage intellectual laziness and tempt us to outsource prayerful study to a machine.

So the better question is not simply, “Should Christians use AI?” Most of us already do, directly or indirectly. The better question is, “What does faithful, truthful and responsible use of AI look like?”

## AI is a tool, not a source of wisdom

Christians have always used tools. We use printed Bibles, concordances, commentaries, Bible dictionaries, study notes, Bible software, search engines, podcasts, sermon libraries and theological databases. None of these tools are neutral in the sense that they have no influence on us. Each one shapes what we notice, how quickly we move and which voices we hear first.

AI belongs in that larger category of tools, but it is more powerful and more dangerous because it feels conversational. A commentary does not pretend to be your tutor. A concordance does not sound pastoral. A search engine does not usually write a polished answer in the tone of a confident teacher. AI does. That makes it useful, but also easy to overtrust.

The Bible’s category for wisdom is not speed, efficiency or fluency. Wisdom begins with the fear of the Lord. It involves truthfulness, humility, discernment, patience and obedience. AI may help us gather material, organise thoughts or test an argument, but it cannot love God for us. It cannot submit to Scripture for us. It cannot pray, repent, worship, shepherd or bear responsibility for what we teach.

That matters because Christian study is not merely information management. It is formation. We are not only trying to produce content. We are trying to become faithful people.

## The benefits of AI for research and study

Used carefully, AI can be genuinely helpful in research and study. It can give a beginner a first map of an unfamiliar topic. It can explain the difference between terms such as justification and sanctification, premillennialism and amillennialism or exegesis and eisegesis. It can suggest possible lines of inquiry, help structure notes and turn scattered thoughts into a workable outline.

For theological students, AI can be useful at the early stage of research. It can help identify the main questions in a debate, suggest search terms, clarify what a view claims and show how two positions differ. It can be particularly helpful when a student knows they do not understand something but does not yet know how to ask the right question.

AI can also assist with accessibility. Some people think clearly but struggle to write. Some have disabilities, fatigue, chronic illness, neurodivergence, limited study time or language barriers. A tool that helps organise ideas, simplify a dense paragraph or produce a study plan can be a genuine mercy. Christians should not dismiss that lightly.

But usefulness is not the same as reliability.

## The risks of AI in research and academia

The most obvious problem is accuracy. AI systems can make mistakes. They can invent references, misrepresent scholars, blend similar ideas or state a disputed claim as if it were settled fact. The danger is not only that AI can be wrong. The danger is that it can be wrong in a polished, confident and plausible way.

That is especially dangerous in academic work. A student may ask AI for sources, receive a convincing bibliography and only later discover that some references do not exist or do not support the claim. A student may use AI to summarise a book they have not read, then unknowingly repeat a distortion. A student may submit work that sounds mature but does not reflect their own understanding.

There is also an integrity issue. Most institutions now distinguish between using AI as a study aid and using AI as a substitute for genuine student work. That distinction is important. Asking AI to explain a concept is different from asking it to write the essay. Asking it to critique your outline is different from letting it produce the argument. Asking it to improve clarity is different from letting it replace your thinking.

For Christians, academic integrity should not be reduced to “What can I get away with?” The deeper question is, “Am I telling the truth about my work?” If an assignment is meant to assess my understanding, then I should not hide the fact that a tool supplied the understanding, structure or wording. Even where AI use is allowed, it should be acknowledged according to the institution’s policy.

The irony is that AI can help students learn, but it can also prevent learning. If I use it to explain, test, question and refine my work, it may strengthen my thinking. If I use it to avoid reading, avoid wrestling with Scripture and avoid forming my own judgment, it will make me weaker.

## AI and sermon preparation

Sermon preparation is one of the areas where Christians need particular care. AI can help a preacher brainstorm sermon titles, structure a passage, identify possible illustrations, create discussion questions or check whether a sermon has a clear main point. Those are useful supports.

But preaching is not content production. A sermon is not merely a religious talk. It is the proclamation of God’s word to particular people in a particular place. That requires prayer, exegesis, pastoral knowledge, theological responsibility and spiritual integrity.

AI does not know your congregation. It does not know the grieving widow, the exhausted parent, the person quietly returning to faith, the teenager questioning Christianity, the long-term member carrying bitterness or the visitor who has never heard the gospel clearly. It can generate applications, but it cannot shepherd people.

There is also a danger that AI-generated sermons will sound biblical while remaining shallow. They may move quickly to general truths without doing the hard work of the passage. They may create neat outlines that are more memorable than faithful. They may smooth out tension, avoid difficult details and produce moral lessons instead of gospel proclamation.

A wise preacher may use AI like an assistant, but never like a preacher. It may suggest questions, but it should not replace exegesis. It may help organise, but it should not supply conviction. It may offer wording, but it should not become the voice of the sermon.

## AI and Bible studies

AI can be very helpful for Bible study material. It can generate observation questions, propose discussion prompts, suggest leader notes, identify repeated words and create different levels of difficulty for mixed groups. It can help a leader move from “I know the passage matters” to “Here is a usable study outline.”

This is especially helpful for churches where volunteer leaders are time-poor. A group leader may work full time, care for family, serve at church and still need to prepare a study. AI can help create a first draft that the leader then checks, edits and prays through.

The danger is that AI can also create Bible studies that look useful but are not deeply rooted in the text. It may ask generic questions that could apply to almost any passage. It may move too quickly to personal application. It may miss the literary context. It may fail to notice how the passage fits within the biblical storyline.

A good Bible study should help people listen to Scripture, not merely discuss spiritual ideas. So AI-generated study material must be checked against the passage. Are the questions actually drawn from the text? Does the study respect context? Does it point to Christ where appropriate without forcing him into the passage artificially? Does it leave room for conviction, repentance, worship and obedience?

AI can draft a Bible study, but it cannot discern whether your group needs more challenge, more comfort or more silence.

## AI and compiling Christian resources

One of the most promising uses of AI is resource compilation. AI can help create reading lists, group articles by topic, summarise long documents, compare denominational statements, identify missing sections in a resource hub and turn scattered notes into a coherent guide.

For a Christian website, this can be extremely useful. It can help compile recommended books, Bible studies, devotional archives, topic pages, glossary entries and church resources. It can also help make material more accessible by creating summaries, beginner pathways and clearer headings.

But resource compilation still needs discernment. AI may recommend books that are popular but not suitable. It may treat all theological traditions as equally interchangeable. It may miss whether a source is Reformed, Catholic, Pentecostal, liberal, conservative, academic or devotional. It may not understand why one reader needs a gentle introduction while another needs a more technical treatment.

Christian resources are never just information. They guide people. They shape doctrine, spiritual habits and pastoral instincts. So every compiled resource needs human judgment. Who is this for? What assumptions does it carry? What tradition does it represent? Is it faithful, useful and appropriate for the reader?

## AI and Logos Bible Software

Logos Bible Software is an interesting case because it shows both the promise and the limits of AI in Christian study. Logos is already a research environment built around Bibles, original-language tools, commentaries, dictionaries, systematic theologies, sermon resources and personal libraries. AI features in that context are different from asking a general chatbot a theological question.

When AI searches your Logos library, summarises resources or helps locate relevant material, it is working from sources you own or can identify. That is a major advantage. A general AI answer may not tell you where its claims came from, or may provide poor references. Logos-based AI tools can help connect you back to actual books, articles and passages.

That does not make the output infallible. A summary is still a summary. A search result is still shaped by the tool. A suggested sermon outline still needs pastoral and exegetical judgment. But the ability to trace answers back to real resources is a significant improvement over free-floating AI responses.

For Bible college students and pastors, the best use of Logos AI is not, “Tell me what to think.” It is, “Help me find where to read.” That keeps the tool in its proper place. AI can surface sources. The student, preacher or teacher must still read, weigh and respond.

## A Christian ethic for using AI

Christians should neither panic nor become naïve. AI is not the Antichrist. It is also not the Holy Spirit. It is a tool created by human beings, trained on human material, shaped by human assumptions and used by human sinners.

A faithful Christian approach might include several commitments.

First, use AI truthfully. Do not present AI work as your own. Do not hide substantial help when it should be acknowledged. Do not use AI to create false expertise.

Second, verify important claims. If an AI tool gives a quote, source, statistic or historical claim, check it. If it summarises a scholar, read the scholar. If it interprets Scripture, test the interpretation against the passage.

Third, protect people. Do not paste sensitive pastoral information, private stories, counselling details or confidential church matters into public AI tools. Efficiency is not worth betraying trust.

Fourth, keep formation central. If AI makes you read less, think less, pray less, listen less or depend less on God, then it is harming you even if it makes you more productive.

Fifth, use AI to serve others, not impress them. A polished article, sermon or Bible study is not automatically faithful. The question is whether it helps people know God, understand Scripture and follow Jesus more deeply.

## So should Christians use AI?

Yes, but not carelessly.

AI can be a helpful assistant for research, study, academic preparation, sermon brainstorming, Bible study development and Christian resource building. It can make good work faster and difficult work more accessible. It can help Christians organise material, sharpen questions and communicate more clearly.

But AI should not replace reading, thinking, prayer, Scripture, community, pastoral discernment or academic integrity. It should not be trusted blindly. It should not become a way to appear wiser than we are. It should not be allowed to form us into people who value speed more than faithfulness.

The Christian question is not whether a tool is impressive. The question is whether it can be received with gratitude, used with wisdom and submitted to the lordship of Christ.

Used well, AI can help us study. Used badly, it can help us pretend we have studied. That difference matters.
