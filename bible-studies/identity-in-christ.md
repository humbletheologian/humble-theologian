# Identity in Christ: A 4-Week Bible Study

This study is written for Christians who want to move beyond a simple slogan such as “my identity is in Christ” and ask what that actually means. The phrase is common in churches, songs and sermons, but it can become vague if we do not let Scripture define it.

The aim is not to deny every other part of who we are. Scripture does not erase our history, personality, relationships, bodies, limitations, cultures, vocations or stories. It does, however, reorders them. In Christ, none of these things gets to have the final word over us. We are not finally defined by success, failure, usefulness, shame, pain, approval, independence, ministry title, relationship status, disability, age, work, past sin or other people’s labels. We belong to God through Christ.

This study is aimed at seasoned Christians, but it is written so newer Christians can participate and stretch. Some questions are straightforward. Others are designed to slow the group down and make people think theologically. A good leader does not need to answer every question. Choose the questions that best serve the group.

## How to use this study

Each session has a main passage, a big idea, context notes, discussion questions, application questions and prayer prompts. Encourage people to read the passage before the group meets. Where possible, ask people to keep a Bible open during discussion.

The goal is not merely to share opinions. The goal is to listen carefully to Scripture, identify our assumptions and respond to God with faith, repentance, worship and obedience.

## Suggested rhythm

- Week 1: Made in the Image of God
- Week 2: New Creation in Christ
- Week 3: Chosen, Adopted and Redeemed
- Week 4: The New Self in Community

## Leader note on tone

Identity can be a sensitive subject. Some people carry deep wounds from family, church, disability, illness, work, rejection, grief, addiction, trauma or shame. Keep the discussion honest, but do not turn the group into a counselling session. Make room for prayer. Encourage people to seek appropriate pastoral care or professional support where needed.

The study should be thoughtful, biblical and pastoral. It should challenge people, but not crush them.

# Week 1: Made in the Image of God

## Main passage

Genesis 1:26–31

## Supporting passages

Psalm 8; Genesis 9:6; James 3:8–10

## Big idea

Human dignity is not earned by intelligence, independence, productivity, beauty, strength, spiritual maturity or usefulness. Human dignity is given by God. Before we ask what it means to be “in Christ,” we need to remember what it means to be human before God.

## Opening question

What labels do people commonly use to measure a person’s worth?

You might think of labels connected to work, appearance, age, education, ability, wealth, relationship status, social confidence, ministry usefulness or perceived success.

## Context notes

Genesis 1 presents human beings as the climax of God’s ordered creation. Men and women are made in God’s image and likeness. This does not mean we are divine. It means humanity has a particular vocation under God. We are made to represent God’s rule within creation, to live in relationship with God, to care for the world and to reflect something of God’s character.

This matters because Christian identity does not begin with sin, even though sin is real. It begins with creation. Human beings are creatures made by God, loved by God and accountable to God. Sin damages and distorts us, but it does not erase the truth that human beings are made in God’s image.

This also means Christian identity cannot be reduced to usefulness. A person does not become more human by being more productive. A person does not become less human through disability, illness, age, dependence, grief, cognitive limitation or social weakness. The image of God is not a trophy for the capable. It is God’s gift to humanity.

## Read the passage

Read Genesis 1:26–31 slowly. Then read it again, listening for what God says about humanity before human beings have done anything.

## Observation questions

1. What words or phrases describe humanity’s place in creation?
2. What responsibilities are given to human beings?
3. What does God call creation after humanity is made?
4. What does the passage say before it says anything about human sin?
5. What is shared by both male and female in this passage?

## Interpretation questions

1. What do you think it means for human beings to be made in the image of God?
2. How does this passage challenge the idea that worth must be earned?
3. Why is it important that human dignity is grounded in God’s action rather than human ability?
4. How might Genesis 1 correct both arrogance and self-hatred?
5. What difference does it make that human beings are creatures, not self-created identities?

## Stretch question

Some Christians speak as though our identity only begins after conversion. How does Genesis 1 help us say something more careful? What is true of all people by creation, and what becomes true of believers in Christ?

## Application questions

1. Which labels are you most tempted to use to measure your own worth?
2. Which labels are you most tempted to use to measure other people?
3. Who in your church, workplace or community is most likely to be treated as less valuable?
4. How might a strong doctrine of the image of God change the way a church treats people who are elderly, disabled, unemployed, socially awkward, single, grieving, poor or dependent on others?
5. What would it look like this week to treat someone as an image-bearer rather than as an interruption, problem or project?

## Practice for the week

Each day, choose one person you might usually overlook. It may be someone at work, church, home or in public. Pray briefly for them and ask God to help you see them as someone made in his image.

## Prayer prompts

- Thank God that human worth is his gift, not our achievement.
- Confess where you have measured yourself or others by worldly labels.
- Ask God to help you see people with truth, humility and love.

## Leader notes

Do not rush too quickly to “identity in Christ.” This first week matters because Christian identity does not cancel creation. Grace restores and renews what sin has damaged. If the group jumps immediately to “but we are sinners,” acknowledge that truth, but bring them back to the order of Scripture: creation, fall, redemption and new creation.

# Week 2: New Creation in Christ

## Main passage

2 Corinthians 5:14–21

## Supporting passages

Galatians 2:20; Romans 6:1–11; Colossians 1:13–14

## Big idea

In Christ, believers are not merely improved versions of their old selves. They belong to the new creation that has begun in Jesus. This does not erase our story, but it changes the lens through which our story is understood.

## Opening question

What are some labels that can stick to a person for years, even after life has changed?

You might think of labels such as failure, divorced, disabled, difficult, outsider, addict, overachiever, disappointment, leader, victim, troublemaker, intelligent, lazy, successful, awkward or broken.

## Context notes

Second Corinthians is a deeply personal letter. Paul is defending the nature of his ministry against critics who judged by outward appearance, status and worldly measures of strength. In this section, Paul explains that Christ’s death and resurrection have changed how he sees people. He no longer regards others “according to the flesh,” that is, according to merely human standards.

The statement “if anyone is in Christ, there is a new creation” is not simply a private self-esteem boost. It is a claim about the new reality God has brought into being through Jesus. The death and resurrection of Christ change how believers understand themselves, each other and the world.

This passage also connects identity with reconciliation. We are not given a new identity so we can become spiritually self-absorbed. We are reconciled to God and entrusted with the message of reconciliation.

## Read the passage

Read 2 Corinthians 5:14–21. Pay attention to what Christ has done, what has changed and what believers are now entrusted to do.

## Observation questions

1. What does Paul say controls or compels him?
2. What does Paul say about Christ’s death and those who belong to him?
3. What changes in the way Paul regards people?
4. What does Paul say God was doing in Christ?
5. What responsibility is given to those who have been reconciled?

## Interpretation questions

1. What does it mean to regard someone “according to the flesh”?
2. How does being “in Christ” change a person’s identity?
3. What does “new creation” mean in this passage? Is it only personal, or bigger than that?
4. Why does Paul connect new identity with reconciliation?
5. How does this passage challenge both pride and shame?

## Stretch question

Paul does not say, “If anyone is in Christ, they should try to reinvent themselves.” He says there is new creation. What is the difference between self-reinvention and new creation?

## Application questions

1. Which old labels still have too much power over the way you see yourself?
2. Which labels do you place on others that make it hard to see them as people for whom Christ died?
3. How might this passage change the way a church welcomes people with complicated stories?
4. What would it look like to live as a reconciled person rather than as someone still trying to prove their worth?
5. Is there someone you need to stop seeing according to old categories?

## Practice for the week

Write down three labels that have shaped you. Then write beside each one: “This is part of my story, but it does not have the final word over me in Christ.” Pray through that list honestly.

## Prayer prompts

- Thank God for reconciling us to himself through Christ.
- Confess where old labels have become stronger in your mind than the gospel.
- Ask God to help you see yourself and others through Christ rather than through worldly measures.
- Pray for courage to live as an agent of reconciliation.

## Leader notes

Be careful not to make “new creation” sound like instant emotional healing. Some labels lose power slowly. Some wounds require time, pastoral care, counselling, repentance or restored relationships. The truth of new creation is not less real because people still struggle. It gives the struggle a new context.

# Week 3: Chosen, Adopted and Redeemed

## Main passage

Ephesians 1:3–14

## Supporting passages

Romans 8:14–17; Galatians 4:4–7; 1 Peter 2:9–10

## Big idea

Christian identity is received before it is achieved. In Christ, believers are blessed, chosen, adopted, redeemed, forgiven, sealed and included in God’s purposes. Our obedience grows from grace. It does not purchase grace.

## Opening question

Where do people usually look for assurance that they matter?

You might think of achievement, family approval, romance, ministry success, work performance, appearance, spiritual discipline, social media response, financial stability or being needed by others.

## Context notes

Ephesians begins with a long sentence of praise. Paul piles up descriptions of what God has done for believers in Christ. The emphasis is not on human achievement, but on God’s gracious initiative. God blesses, chooses, adopts, redeems, forgives, reveals, includes and seals.

This passage is sometimes pulled into debates about election and predestination. Those debates matter, but they should not cause us to miss the pastoral force of the passage. Paul wants believers to know that their identity rests in God’s gracious action in Christ. They are not spiritual orphans trying to earn a place at the table.

The passage is also deeply Trinitarian. The Father blesses and adopts, the Son redeems through his blood and the Spirit seals believers as a guarantee of their inheritance. Christian identity is not a motivational technique. It is grounded in the saving work of the triune God.

## Read the passage

Read Ephesians 1:3–14. As you read, list every phrase that describes what God has done for believers in Christ.

## Observation questions

1. What repeated phrases or themes do you notice?
2. What blessings are described in the passage?
3. What does Paul say about adoption?
4. What role does redemption and forgiveness play?
5. What does Paul say about the Holy Spirit?

## Interpretation questions

1. Why does Paul begin with praise before giving instructions?
2. What does adoption add to our understanding of Christian identity?
3. How does redemption address shame, guilt and bondage?
4. What does it mean to be sealed with the Holy Spirit?
5. How does this passage challenge the belief that we must earn our place with God?

## Stretch question

This passage speaks about blessing, adoption and inheritance. How can Christians receive these truths without turning them into entitlement, superiority or spiritual arrogance?

## Application questions

1. Which part of this passage is easiest for you to believe?
2. Which part is hardest for you to believe personally?
3. Do you tend to live more like an adopted child or like someone still trying to earn a place in the family?
4. How might this passage reshape prayer, worship and service?
5. How should adoption in Christ affect the way believers treat one another in the church?

## Practice for the week

Choose one phrase from Ephesians 1:3–14 and carry it with you for the week. Do not choose the phrase that sounds most impressive. Choose the one you most need to receive.

For example:

- blessed in Christ
- chosen in Christ
- adopted through Jesus Christ
- redeemed through his blood
- forgiven according to grace
- sealed with the promised Holy Spirit

## Prayer prompts

- Praise God for the grace described in Ephesians 1.
- Thank God that identity in Christ is received, not achieved.
- Confess where you have treated grace as something to earn.
- Ask God to help the church live as a family, not a competition.

## Leader notes

Some people may struggle with adoption language because of painful family experiences. Do not minimise that. The point is not that every earthly family gives us a good picture of God. The point is that God’s fatherly welcome in Christ is deeper, purer and more faithful than even the best human family.

# Week 4: The New Self in Community

## Main passage

Colossians 3:1–17

## Supporting passages

Ephesians 4:17–32; Romans 12:1–8; Philippians 2:1–11

## Big idea

Identity in Christ is not only something we claim. It is something we learn to live. Because believers have died and been raised with Christ, they are called to put off the old self and put on the new self in community.

## Opening question

What is the difference between saying “this is who I am” and being formed into the likeness of Christ?

## Context notes

Colossians 3 moves from identity to practice. Paul does not say, “Try hard so that God will accept you.” He says believers have died, their life is hidden with Christ in God and Christ is their life. Because that is true, they are to put to death what belongs to the old life and put on the character of the new life.

This means Christian identity is not passive. It is not an excuse to avoid growth. Grace does not leave us unchanged. It trains us to become who we already are in Christ.

The passage is also communal. Paul addresses the church, not merely isolated individuals. Compassion, kindness, humility, meekness, patience, forgiveness, love, peace, thankfulness, worship and teaching are all lived with other people. Identity in Christ creates a new community where old dividing labels lose their power.

## Read the passage

Read Colossians 3:1–17. Notice the movement from what is true of believers in Christ to how believers are called to live together.

## Observation questions

1. What does Paul say is already true of believers?
2. What are believers told to put to death?
3. What are believers told to put on?
4. What social or cultural divisions are named in the passage?
5. What role do forgiveness, love, peace and thankfulness play?

## Interpretation questions

1. Why does Paul ground ethical commands in union with Christ?
2. What does it mean that our life is hidden with Christ in God?
3. How does the “new self” differ from self-improvement?
4. Why does Paul mention divisions such as Greek and Jew, circumcised and uncircumcised, barbarian, Scythian, slave and free?
5. How does Christian community test whether our claimed identity is actually shaping us?

## Stretch question

Some people use “identity” language to avoid responsibility: “This is just who I am.” Others use spiritual growth language to deny grace: “I am only valuable if I improve.” How does Colossians 3 challenge both errors?

## Application questions

1. What part of the old self do you most need to put off?
2. Which virtue in this passage do you most need to put on?
3. How does your church community help or hinder people in living out their identity in Christ?
4. Are there labels in your church that quietly create insiders and outsiders?
5. What would change if “Christ is all, and in all” became a lived reality rather than a slogan?

## Practice for the week

Choose one “put off” and one “put on” from Colossians 3. Be specific.

For example:

- Put off resentment. Put on forgiveness.
- Put off harsh speech. Put on kindness.
- Put off comparison. Put on thankfulness.
- Put off suspicion. Put on love.

At the end of each day, ask: Where did I resist the old self today? Where did I cooperate with the Spirit’s formation of the new self?

## Prayer prompts

- Thank God that your life is hidden with Christ.
- Confess the habits of the old self that still cling closely.
- Ask the Spirit to form the character of Christ in you.
- Pray for your church to become a community where worldly labels lose their power.

## Leader notes

This session should not become moralism. The commands matter, but they flow from grace. Keep asking: What has God already done in Christ? What is therefore true of us? How are we now called to live?

# Final group reflection

After completing the four sessions, spend time reflecting on the whole study.

## Review questions

1. What has changed in the way you understand identity in Christ?
2. Which passage challenged you most?
3. Which truth do you most need to keep returning to?
4. What label has lost some power over you?
5. How can this group help one another live from identity in Christ rather than from fear, shame, pride or comparison?

## A closing prayer

Father, thank you that our worth is not something we create, perform or defend. Thank you that we are made in your image and that, in Christ, we are reconciled, adopted, redeemed and made new. Teach us to see ourselves truthfully, to see others with grace and to live as people whose lives are hidden with Christ in you. By your Spirit, help us put off the old self and put on the new, for the sake of your church and the glory of Jesus. Amen.
