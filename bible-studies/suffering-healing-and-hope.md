# Suffering, Healing and Hope: A 4-Week Bible Study

This study is written for thoughtful Christians, group leaders and readers who want to take suffering and healing seriously without turning either into a slogan. It is suitable for seasoned Christians, but it is also written so newer Christians can stretch beyond easy answers without being crushed by complexity.

The aim is not to settle every question about why God heals some people and not others. The aim is to listen carefully to Scripture, pray honestly and learn to hold together three truths that Christians sometimes separate: God is good, suffering is real and resurrection hope is not escapism.

This study may be especially useful for groups discussing disability, illness, chronic pain, grief, unanswered prayer or pastoral care. It should be handled gently. Some people in the room may be living the questions, not merely discussing them.

## How to use this study

Each week includes a main passage, supporting passages, context notes, questions and a suggested practice. Leaders do not need to ask every question. Choose the questions that best serve the group. Leave space for silence, prayer and honest disagreement.

A helpful rhythm is:

1. Begin with prayer.
2. Read the passage slowly.
3. Notice what the text says before rushing to application.
4. Let people answer honestly, not perform spiritually.
5. End with prayer that is truthful rather than polished.

## Week 1: When Suffering Does Not Fit Our Simple Answers

**Main passage:** John 9:1–12  
**Supporting passages:** Job 1:20–22; Psalm 13; Romans 8:18–25

### Big idea

Jesus refuses to reduce suffering to a simple blame game. Some suffering is connected to human sin in a general fallen-world sense, but not every instance of suffering can be traced to a particular personal failure.

### Context notes

In John 9, Jesus and his disciples encounter a man who was blind from birth. The disciples ask whose sin caused the man’s blindness, his own or his parents’. Their question assumes that suffering must have a neat moral explanation. Jesus rejects the premise. He does not say suffering is good. He does not deny that sin has damaged creation. But he refuses to treat the man as a theological puzzle or a punishment.

This matters pastorally. Christians can sometimes speak as if every disability, illness or hardship must reveal a lack of faith, hidden sin, demonic attack or failure to pray properly. John 9 should make us cautious. Jesus sees the person before he answers the question.

### Opening question

What are some common explanations Christians give when someone suffers? Which ones are helpful and which ones can be harmful?

### Observation questions

1. What question do the disciples ask Jesus in John 9:2?
2. What assumptions seem to sit behind their question?
3. How does Jesus answer them?
4. What does Jesus do before the man fully understands who he is?
5. How do the neighbours respond when the man is healed?

### Interpretation questions

1. Why is it significant that Jesus rejects the disciples’ either/or explanation?
2. Does Jesus’ answer mean God caused the man’s blindness simply so the miracle could happen? Why should we be careful with that reading?
3. How does this passage challenge the habit of treating disabled people mainly as examples, lessons or problems to solve?
4. What does this passage teach us about the difference between theological curiosity and pastoral attention?

### Stretch question

Some translations of John 9:3 can sound as if the man was born blind in order that God’s works would later be displayed. Others understand Jesus as shifting the focus away from causation and toward what God is about to do. How does this affect the way we speak about suffering?

### Application questions

1. Have you ever heard suffering explained in a way that sounded too neat?
2. How can Christians avoid turning people’s pain into a theological lecture?
3. What would it look like for a church to see the person before trying to explain the problem?
4. How might this passage shape the way we speak to someone who is living with disability, illness or grief?

### Weekly practice

This week, pay attention to how often people try to explain suffering quickly. When you notice it, pause and pray: “Lord, help me to see the person before I reach for an answer.”

### Prayer prompt

Father, forgive us for the times we have explained people instead of loving them. Teach us to see with the compassion of Jesus. Give us wisdom where answers are needed and silence where presence is better. Amen.

### Leader notes

Be careful not to let this session become theoretical. Some group members may carry stories of being blamed for their suffering. Make room for lament. Do not force people to share personal pain. If someone does share, thank them and avoid correcting them too quickly.

## Week 2: Jesus, Healing and the Kingdom

**Main passage:** Mark 1:29–45  
**Supporting passages:** Matthew 11:2–6; Luke 4:16–21; Acts 3:1–10

### Big idea

Jesus’ healings are signs of the kingdom of God. They show God’s compassion and point forward to the restoration of creation, but they do not mean every faithful person will be fully healed in this age.

### Context notes

Mark’s Gospel presents Jesus as announcing the kingdom of God in word and deed. He teaches with authority, casts out demons, heals the sick and restores people to community. Healing is not a side issue in Jesus’ ministry. It is one of the ways the kingdom becomes visible.

At the same time, the New Testament does not present this present age as if all suffering has already disappeared. The kingdom has come in Jesus, but it has not yet arrived in fullness. Christians live between resurrection and renewal, between the firstfruits of the Spirit and the redemption of our bodies.

This helps us avoid two errors. One error is to stop expecting God to heal. The other is to assume that continued suffering proves spiritual failure.

### Opening question

When Christians talk about healing, what extremes have you seen or heard?

### Observation questions

1. Who does Jesus heal in Mark 1:29–34?
2. What does Jesus do early the next morning?
3. How do the disciples respond when people are looking for him?
4. What does Jesus say his mission is in Mark 1:38?
5. How does Jesus respond to the man with leprosy?

### Interpretation questions

1. What do Jesus’ healings reveal about God’s character?
2. Why does Jesus not simply stay in one place healing everyone who comes to him?
3. How do healing, preaching and prayer relate in this passage?
4. What does Jesus’ willingness to touch the man with leprosy show about compassion, purity and social exclusion?

### Stretch question

If Jesus’ miracles are signs of the kingdom, what happens when Christians turn them into formulas? What is lost when healing becomes a technique rather than a gift of God’s mercy?

### Application questions

1. How can churches pray boldly for healing without making promises God has not made?
2. How can we honour testimonies of healing without implying that people who are not healed have failed?
3. What might it mean to seek healing and belonging, not only healing instead of belonging?
4. How can a church become a place where people who remain disabled, sick or grieving are still fully valued?

### Weekly practice

Pray for someone who needs healing, but do it with humility. Ask God for healing, comfort, endurance, wise medical care, community support and hope. Notice whether your prayer is broad enough to love the whole person.

### Prayer prompt

Lord Jesus, you had compassion on the sick, the excluded and the weary. Teach us to pray with faith and humility. Give us courage to ask for healing and love to walk with people whether healing comes quickly, slowly or not in the way we hoped. Amen.

### Leader notes

Avoid allowing this week to become a debate about whether miracles still happen. The study assumes God can heal and does heal, but it also asks how we care for people when healing is partial, delayed or absent. Keep the conversation pastoral.

## Week 3: Lament, Weakness and Unanswered Prayer

**Main passage:** 2 Corinthians 12:1–10  
**Supporting passages:** Psalm 22:1–5; Matthew 26:36–46; Romans 8:26–27

### Big idea

Faithful prayer does not always remove suffering. Sometimes God’s grace meets us in weakness rather than by removing weakness immediately.

### Context notes

In 2 Corinthians 12, Paul speaks about a “thorn in the flesh.” Christians have debated what this thorn was. The text does not tell us. That may be part of its usefulness. The point is not to identify Paul’s condition but to notice how God responds.

Paul pleads three times for the thorn to be removed. God’s answer is not the answer Paul asked for: “My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness.” This does not make suffering good. It does not mean Christians should stop asking for deliverance. But it does show that God’s power is not limited to visible strength, public success or bodily ease.

The Bible also gives us the language of lament. Lament is not unbelief. It is faith refusing to pretend. The Psalms teach God’s people to bring grief, confusion and complaint into the presence of God.

### Opening question

Why do Christians sometimes find lament uncomfortable?

### Observation questions

1. What does Paul ask God to remove?
2. How many times does he plead with the Lord?
3. What answer does he receive?
4. How does Paul describe weakness after hearing God’s answer?
5. What kinds of weakness or hardship does he name in verse 10?

### Interpretation questions

1. Why is it important that Paul prays for the thorn to be removed?
2. What does this passage teach us about unanswered prayer?
3. How is God’s power made perfect in weakness?
4. How does this passage challenge churches that confuse strength with impressiveness?

### Stretch question

Paul does not glorify suffering for its own sake. He glories in weakness because Christ’s power rests on him. How can we tell the difference between a Christian theology of weakness and unhealthy resignation?

### Application questions

1. Where are you tempted to interpret unanswered prayer as God’s absence?
2. How might lament help Christians pray more honestly?
3. What kinds of weakness do churches often hide or undervalue?
4. How can a group support someone whose suffering has not been removed?

### Weekly practice

Write a short prayer of lament. Use this pattern if helpful:

- God, this is what hurts.
- This is what I do not understand.
- This is what I am asking you to do.
- This is what I still believe about you.

You do not need to share it unless you choose to.

### Prayer prompt

God of mercy, teach us to pray honestly. When healing does not come as quickly as we hope, keep us from despair. When weakness remains, let the power of Christ rest on us. When we do not know how to pray, help us by your Spirit. Amen.

### Leader notes

This session may bring up grief, disappointment or anger. Do not rush to defend God with simplistic answers. Scripture itself gives us lament. Let the Bible give permission for honest prayer.

## Week 4: Resurrection Hope and Embodied Belonging

**Main passage:** 1 Corinthians 15:35–58  
**Supporting passages:** Revelation 21:1–5; Luke 24:36–43; Philippians 3:20–21

### Big idea

Christian hope is not escape from the body but the resurrection and renewal of embodied life. This hope should make Christians steadfast, loving and present now.

### Context notes

In 1 Corinthians 15, Paul defends the resurrection of the dead. Christian hope is not that souls escape the body forever. It is that God raises the dead and transforms creation. Jesus’ resurrection is the firstfruits of what God will do for those who belong to Christ.

This matters for suffering and disability. Resurrection hope does not mean bodies do not matter now. It means bodies matter so much that God intends to redeem them. It also means we should not treat disabled or suffering bodies as disposable, embarrassing or spiritually inferior.

The resurrection body will be transformed, but it will still be embodied life. Jesus’ risen body is recognisable, touchable and capable of eating. The scars of crucifixion are not erased from the story of redemption. That does not answer every question about disability and resurrection, but it does caution us against imagining hope as the rejection of embodied people.

### Opening question

When people talk about heaven, do they usually speak as if the body matters or as if the body is left behind?

### Observation questions

1. What question does Paul answer in 1 Corinthians 15:35?
2. What images does Paul use to describe resurrection transformation?
3. What contrasts does Paul make between the present body and the resurrection body?
4. What victory does God give through Christ?
5. What does Paul say should result from resurrection hope in verse 58?

### Interpretation questions

1. Why is resurrection better than mere escape from suffering?
2. How does resurrection hope honour the body without idolising present health or ability?
3. What does verse 58 suggest about the connection between future hope and present work?
4. How might resurrection hope change the way churches value people whose bodies are weak, disabled, painful or ageing?

### Stretch question

Some Christians assume resurrection means all traces of disability disappear. Others wonder whether certain aspects of disabled embodiment may be part of a person’s identity and story. What can we say with confidence, and where should we remain humble?

### Application questions

1. How does resurrection hope comfort people who suffer?
2. How does it challenge churches that only value visible strength and productivity?
3. What does it mean to be “steadfast, immovable, always abounding in the work of the Lord” without burning people out?
4. How can a group practise embodied belonging now?

### Weekly practice

Choose one practical act of embodied care this week. Visit, call, include, adjust, make space, offer transport, pray with someone or remove a barrier. Let resurrection hope become concrete love.

### Prayer prompt

Risen Lord Jesus, you are the firstfruits of new creation. Teach us to hope without escaping the present and to serve without despair. Help us honour bodies now as we wait for the redemption of all things. Amen.

### Leader notes

This week should end with hope, not triumphalism. Resurrection hope is strong enough to face suffering honestly. Avoid speculating too confidently about exactly what resurrection bodies will be like. Keep the emphasis on Christ’s victory, embodied hope and faithful love now.
