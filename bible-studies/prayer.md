# Prayer: A 4-Week Bible Study

Prayer is one of the most familiar parts of Christian life, but it is also one of the easiest to misunderstand. Some Christians think of prayer mainly as asking God for help. Others treat it as a spiritual discipline they know they should practise but quietly feel guilty about. Some pray easily in groups, while others freeze when asked to pray aloud. Some have kept prayer journals for years, while others wonder whether their prayers are too simple, too scattered or too distracted to count.

This study begins with a different assumption. Prayer is not a performance. Prayer is not a technique for controlling God. Prayer is not a way to sound spiritual in front of others. Prayer is the gift of speaking with the living God, who has made himself known as Father, Son and Holy Spirit.

That means prayer is relational before it is practical. It includes asking, but it is more than asking. It includes words, but it is more than words. It includes private devotion, but it also belongs in the gathered life of the church. Through prayer we bring ourselves before God, confess our dependence, praise his goodness, intercede for others, lament what is broken, seek wisdom, receive mercy and are slowly shaped into people who trust him.

This study is aimed at seasoned Christians, group leaders and thoughtful believers who want to go deeper. Newer Christians should still be able to participate, but some questions are designed to stretch people beyond basic answers. Leaders should feel free to choose the questions that best serve the group.

## How to use this study

Each week includes a main passage, supporting passages, context notes, questions and a simple practice. The goal is not to rush through every question. The goal is to listen to Scripture, reflect honestly and practise prayer together.

If the group includes newer Christians, define unfamiliar words as they arise. If the group includes mature Christians, resist the temptation to answer too quickly. Prayer is not mastered by knowing the right vocabulary. It is learned by dependence, humility and practice.

## Suggested rhythm

- Week 1: Lord, Teach Us to Pray
- Week 2: Praying to the Triune God
- Week 3: The Many Languages of Prayer
- Week 4: Perseverance, Prayer Journals and Praise Reports

# Week 1: Lord, Teach Us to Pray

## Main passage

Luke 11:1–13

## Supporting passages

Matthew 6:5–15; Psalm 63:1–8; Hebrews 4:14–16

## Big idea

Prayer is learned from Jesus. It begins with trust in God as Father and grows through dependence, worship, confession and persistence.

## Opening question

When you hear the word prayer, what comes to mind first: comfort, guilt, habit, confusion, duty, intimacy or something else?

## Context notes

Luke tells us that Jesus was praying when one of his disciples asked, “Lord, teach us to pray.” That request matters. The disciples had grown up in a praying culture. They knew synagogue prayers, psalms and Jewish patterns of devotion. Yet being around Jesus made them realise that prayer was not merely something to recite. It was a life of communion with God.

Jesus does not respond by giving them a complicated technique. He gives them a simple, God-centred pattern. Prayer begins with God’s name, God’s kingdom and daily dependence. It includes forgiveness received and forgiveness extended. It asks for protection in temptation. It is both reverent and intimate.

Jesus then gives two short illustrations. One emphasises boldness and persistence. The other emphasises the Father’s goodness. The point is not that God is reluctant and must be worn down. The point is that disciples can come to God with confidence because he is better than even the best human parent.

## Read the passage

Read Luke 11:1–13 slowly. Listen for what Jesus teaches about God, not just what he teaches about prayer.

## Observation questions

1. What prompts the disciples’ request?
2. What comes first in the prayer Jesus teaches?
3. What everyday needs are included?
4. What does the prayer assume about sin and forgiveness?
5. What does Jesus say about asking, seeking and knocking?

## Interpretation questions

1. Why does Jesus teach us to begin with God’s name and kingdom before our own requests?
2. What does the word Father communicate about Christian prayer?
3. How does the Lord’s Prayer hold together worship, dependence, forgiveness and spiritual protection?
4. What is the difference between persistence in prayer and trying to pressure God?
5. How does this passage challenge the idea that prayer must sound impressive to be faithful?

## Stretch question

Compare Luke 11:1–13 with Matthew 6:5–15. What does Matthew add about hypocrisy, public performance and empty repetition?

## Application questions

1. Which part of the Lord’s Prayer do you most naturally pray?
2. Which part do you tend to neglect?
3. How might your prayer life change if God’s kingdom came before your immediate concerns?
4. What would it look like to pray with more trust rather than more religious pressure?

## Practice for the week

Pray the Lord’s Prayer once a day, slowly. Pause after each line and turn it into your own prayer. For example, after “Give us each day our daily bread,” name your dependence before God without pretending to be more self-sufficient than you are.

## Prayer prompt

Father, teach us to pray as disciples of Jesus. Free us from performance, guilt and empty words. Give us trust, reverence and dependence.

## Leader notes

Some people may have complicated experiences with the word Father. Do not minimise that. The point is not that every earthly father reflects God well. The point is that God reveals true fatherhood, and all human fatherhood is judged by him. Keep the group centred on the character of God revealed in Jesus.

# Week 2: Praying to the Triune God

## Main passage

Ephesians 2:13–22

## Supporting passages

Romans 8:14–27; John 14:12–17; Hebrews 7:23–25; Revelation 5:8–14

## Big idea

Christian prayer is normally offered to the Father, through the Son and by the Holy Spirit, yet Scripture also shows believers calling on Jesus. Prayer is deeply Trinitarian because salvation is deeply Trinitarian.

## Opening question

When you pray, do you usually address the Father, Jesus, the Holy Spirit or simply “God”? Why?

## Context notes

Many Christians pray without thinking much about which member of the Trinity they are addressing. That is not necessarily wrong. God is one. Yet Scripture gives us a rich pattern for prayer.

Ephesians 2 says that through Christ both Jews and Gentiles have access in one Spirit to the Father. This is one of the clearest summaries of Christian prayer. We come to the Father because the Son has reconciled us and the Spirit gives us access. Prayer is not forcing our way into God’s presence. It is entering by grace.

The New Testament often directs prayer to the Father. Jesus teaches his disciples to pray, “Father.” Yet the early Christians also call on the name of the Lord Jesus. Stephen prays, “Lord Jesus, receive my spirit” and “Lord, do not hold this sin against them.” The book of Revelation pictures worship offered to the one seated on the throne and to the Lamb.

What about praying to the Holy Spirit? The New Testament more often speaks of praying in or by the Spirit than praying to the Spirit. Still, because the Spirit is fully God, prayer to the Spirit is not inappropriate. A simple way to think about it is this: Christian prayer is most commonly to the Father, through the Son, by the Spirit, but because God is Triune, Christians may also address the Son and the Spirit in worship, dependence and need.

## Read the passage

Read Ephesians 2:13–22. Notice the movement: brought near by Christ, reconciled through the cross, access in the Spirit to the Father and built together as God’s dwelling.

## Observation questions

1. What has Christ done for those who were far away?
2. What does the passage say about peace and reconciliation?
3. Who has access to the Father?
4. How is the Spirit described in relation to God’s people?
5. What communal language does Paul use?

## Interpretation questions

1. Why is access to God a gift rather than an achievement?
2. What does it mean to pray to the Father through the Son?
3. How does the Holy Spirit help us pray according to Romans 8:26–27?
4. Why does Trinitarian prayer matter for Christians who feel unworthy or spiritually weak?
5. How does this passage challenge overly individualistic ideas of prayer?

## Stretch question

Read Romans 8:14–27. What does this passage teach about adoption, groaning, weakness and the Spirit’s help in prayer?

## Application questions

1. How might your prayer change if you remembered that access to God is already given in Christ?
2. When do you most need the Spirit’s help because you do not know how to pray?
3. What would it look like for your group or church to pray as people being built together, not just as private individuals?
4. Are there ways your prayers could become more consciously Trinitarian without becoming forced or formulaic?

## Practice for the week

Begin some prayers this week with a simple Trinitarian pattern:

Father, I come to you through Jesus your Son. Holy Spirit, help me pray with honesty, faith and wisdom.

Then pray naturally. The pattern is a doorway, not a script you must keep repeating.

## Prayer prompt

Father, thank you that we have access to you through Jesus and in the Spirit. Teach us to pray as beloved children, not anxious outsiders.

## Leader notes

This week may feel more theological than practical, but it is deeply pastoral. People who think prayer depends on their worthiness need to see that Christian prayer depends on Christ. People who feel too weak to pray need to hear that the Spirit helps us in weakness.

# Week 3: The Many Languages of Prayer

## Main passage

Philippians 4:4–9

## Supporting passages

Psalm 13; Psalm 51; Psalm 103; 1 Timothy 2:1–6; James 5:13–18; Nehemiah 1:4–11

## Big idea

Prayer includes praise, thanksgiving, confession, lament, petition and intercession. Mature prayer does not flatten life. It brings joy, sin, grief, anxiety, need and hope before God.

## Opening question

Which type of prayer comes most naturally to you: praise, confession, thanks, lament, asking for yourself or praying for others?

## Context notes

Philippians 4 is often quoted in anxious moments, and rightly so. Paul tells believers not to be anxious about anything, but in everything, by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving, to let their requests be made known to God. Yet this is not a shallow command to “just stop worrying.” Paul writes from imprisonment to a church facing pressure. His words are grounded in the nearness of the Lord.

The Bible gives us many languages for prayer. Praise names God’s character and goodness. Thanksgiving remembers his gifts. Confession tells the truth about sin. Lament brings grief, confusion and pain to God instead of pretending they do not exist. Petition asks God for our own needs. Intercession asks God to act for others. Prayer may be quiet, spoken, written, sung, desperate, joyful, structured or spontaneous.

Seasoned Christians sometimes become narrow in prayer without realising it. We may only ask. Or only thank. Or only pray safe, general prayers. The Psalms stretch us because they teach us to bring the whole of life before God. They are not embarrassed by tears, anger, fear, repentance, praise or longing.

## Read the passage

Read Philippians 4:4–9. Notice the connection between rejoicing, gentleness, prayer, thanksgiving, peace and disciplined thought.

## Observation questions

1. What commands does Paul give?
2. What does he say about anxiety?
3. What kinds of prayer language appear in the passage?
4. What is promised in relation to the peace of God?
5. What does Paul say about the mind?

## Interpretation questions

1. Why does Paul connect prayer with thanksgiving even when people are anxious?
2. What is the difference between denying anxiety and bringing anxiety to God?
3. How does the peace of God guard hearts and minds without necessarily removing every problem?
4. Why does prayer need both honesty and trust?
5. How do the Psalms help us pray with more emotional and spiritual range?

## Stretch question

Read Psalm 13. How does the psalm move from complaint to trust? What does it teach us about lament as faithful prayer?

## Types of prayer to discuss

### Praise

Prayer that honours God for who he is.

### Thanksgiving

Prayer that remembers and names God’s gifts.

### Confession

Prayer that tells the truth about sin and receives mercy.

### Lament

Prayer that brings grief, confusion and pain before God.

### Petition

Prayer that asks God for our own needs.

### Intercession

Prayer that asks God to act for others.

### Listening and surrender

Prayer that quiets our self-importance and yields to God’s will.

## Application questions

1. Which type of prayer is most common in your personal prayer life?
2. Which type is least common?
3. What might your prayers reveal about your view of God?
4. Where might you need to learn lament rather than rushing to optimism?
5. Who or what has God placed on your heart for intercession?

## Practice for the week

Choose one day this week to pray through six movements:

1. Praise God for who he is.
2. Thank God for one specific gift.
3. Confess one sin or distorted desire.
4. Lament one grief or burden.
5. Ask God for one personal need.
6. Intercede for one person, church or situation.

Keep it simple. The goal is not length. The goal is a fuller conversation with God.

## Prayer prompt

Lord, teach us to bring our whole lives before you. Receive our praise, thanks, confession, grief, needs and intercession. Shape our hearts in your peace.

## Leader notes

Be careful with lament. Some groups rush too quickly to fix pain or explain it. Let Scripture give permission for honest prayer. At the same time, keep lament directed toward God rather than allowing the discussion to become aimless venting.

# Week 4: Perseverance, Prayer Journals and Praise Reports

## Main passage

Luke 18:1–8

## Supporting passages

1 Thessalonians 5:16–18; 1 John 5:13–15; 2 Corinthians 12:7–10; James 5:13–18; Psalm 77

## Big idea

Prayer is sustained by faith in God’s character, not by visible results alone. Journals and praise reports can help us remember, but they should serve prayer rather than turn prayer into record-keeping or pressure.

## Opening question

What helps you keep praying when an answer seems delayed, unclear or different from what you hoped?

## Context notes

Luke introduces the parable of the persistent widow by telling us its purpose: Jesus wants his disciples always to pray and not lose heart. The widow is vulnerable. The judge is unjust. She keeps coming because she has nowhere else to go. Eventually the judge grants justice, not because he is good, but because she persists.

Jesus’ point is not that God is like the unjust judge. The contrast is the point. If even an unjust judge may eventually act, how much more will the righteous God hear his chosen ones who cry to him? Prayer continues because God is just, attentive and faithful, even when his timing is not ours.

This is where prayer journals and praise reports can help. A prayer journal can record requests, Scripture, burdens, answered prayers and patterns of growth. Praise reports help individuals and churches remember that God has been at work. They turn vague gratitude into specific testimony.

But these tools need wisdom. A journal is not a spiritual scorecard. A praise report should not imply that God is present only when circumstances improve. Sometimes God answers with yes. Sometimes with no. Sometimes with wait. Sometimes with grace sufficient for weakness. Paul’s thorn in the flesh reminds us that unanswered or differently answered prayer is not necessarily failed prayer.

## Read the passage

Read Luke 18:1–8. Notice what Jesus says about persistence, justice and faith.

## Observation questions

1. Why does Luke say Jesus told this parable?
2. What kind of judge is described?
3. What does the widow keep seeking?
4. How does Jesus contrast God with the judge?
5. What question does Jesus ask at the end?

## Interpretation questions

1. Why is it important to see contrast, not similarity, between God and the unjust judge?
2. What does this parable teach about persistence without making prayer mechanical?
3. How can Christians keep praying without pretending delay is easy?
4. How does 2 Corinthians 12:7–10 complicate simplistic ideas of answered prayer?
5. What does faithfulness in prayer look like when there is no neat resolution?

## Stretch question

Read Psalm 77. How does the psalmist move from distress to remembering God’s works? How might this shape the use of prayer journals and praise reports?

## Prayer journals

A prayer journal can include:

- passages of Scripture that shape prayer
- people or situations you are praying for
- honest reflections, including confusion and grief
- specific requests
- ways God seems to be answering or forming you
- praise reports and moments of gratitude

The value of a journal is not that it makes prayer more impressive. It helps us notice patterns, remember God’s faithfulness and pray with greater attentiveness.

## Praise reports

Praise reports are testimonies of God’s kindness, provision, comfort, conviction, healing, guidance or sustaining grace. They are useful because churches easily remember problems and forget grace.

However, praise reports should be handled pastorally. If we only share dramatic breakthroughs, people with long-term suffering may feel invisible. It is good to praise God for healing, provision and open doors. It is also good to praise God for endurance, comfort, repentance, reconciliation, courage and grace in weakness.

## Application questions

1. Where are you tempted to give up praying?
2. Where might you need to pray with more persistence and less demand?
3. How could a prayer journal help you remember rather than merely record?
4. What kinds of praise reports does your church tend to celebrate?
5. Are there quieter works of God your group needs to notice more often?

## Practice for the week

Start a simple prayer journal for seven days. Use four headings:

- Scripture
- Prayer
- People
- Praise

Write briefly. At the end of the week, look back and ask: What did I keep bringing to God? What did I avoid bringing to God? Where did I notice grace?

## Prayer prompt

God of mercy and justice, teach us to pray and not lose heart. Help us remember your faithfulness, trust your timing and recognise your grace even when answers are not simple.

## Leader notes

This week may surface disappointment with prayer. Do not rush to defend God with shallow answers. Let Scripture hold together confidence and mystery. Encourage honest prayer, patient trust and a broader view of God’s work.

# Closing Reflection

Prayer is not one more religious task to add to an already crowded life. It is the way children speak to their Father, disciples depend on their Lord and weak people receive help from the Spirit. Prayer does not make us impressive. It makes us honest. It teaches us to live before God rather than before the audience in our own minds.

A mature prayer life will usually become both simpler and deeper over time. Simpler, because we learn that God is not impressed by religious performance. Deeper, because more of life is brought into his presence. Praise, confession, lament, petition, intercession, silence, thanksgiving, journals and praise reports all have their place. But the heart of prayer is God himself.
